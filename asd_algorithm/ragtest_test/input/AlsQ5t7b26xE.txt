# Depression pathophysiology, risk prediction of recurrence and comorbid psychiatric disorders using genome-wide analyses

Received: 30 June 2022

Accepted: 17 April 2023

# A list of authors and their affiliations appears at the end of the paper

Published online: 18 July 2023

# Check for updates

Depression is a common psychiatric disorder and a leading cause of disability worldwide. Here we conducted a genome-wide association study meta-analysis of six datasets, including ${ \tt > } 1 . 3$ million individuals (371,184 with depression) and identifed 243 risk loci. Overall, 64 loci were new, including genes encoding glutamate and GABA receptors, which are targets for antidepressant drugs. Intersection with functional genomics data prioritized likely causal genes and revealed new enrichment of prenatal GABAergic neurons, astrocytes and oligodendrocyte lineages. We found depression to be highly polygenic, with \~11,700 variants explaining $90 \%$ of the single-nucleotide polymorphism heritability, estimating that $5 9 5 \%$ of risk variants for other psychiatric disorders (anxiety, schizophrenia, bipolar disorder and attention defcit hyperactivity disorder) were infuencing depression risk when both concordant and discordant variants were considered, and nearly all depression risk variants infuenced educational attainment. Additionally, depression genetic risk was associated with impaired complex cognition domains. We dissected the genetic and clinical heterogeneity, revealing distinct polygenic architectures across subgroups of depression and demonstrating signifcantly increased absolute risks for recurrence and psychiatric comorbidity among cases of depression with the highest polygenic burden, with considerable sex diferences. The risks were up to 5- and 32-fold higher than cases with the lowest polygenic burden and the background population, respectively. These results deepen the understanding of the biology underlying depression, its disease progression and inform precision medicine approaches to treatment.

Depression (DEP) is a genetic and phenotypic complex psychiatric disorder with a lifetime prevalence of $1 5 \mathrm { - } 2 0 \% ^ { 1 - 3 }$ . It is often recurrent and accompanied by considerable morbidity and comorbidity, excess mortality, increased risk of suicide and substantial costs worldwide4–8 . Individuals diagnosed with DEP have an increased risk of developing practically all other types of mental disorders, in particular anxiety (ANX), bipolar disorder (BP), schizophrenia (SZ) and substance use disorder (SUD)9,10. This relationship is generally bi-directional (with significantly increased risk of comorbidity between DEP and other types of psychiatric disorders, regardless of whether DEP is diagnosed before or after a comorbid psychiatric disorder).

Heritability estimates based on twin studies $( h ^ { 2 } = 0 . 3 7 )$ have indicated that familial aggregation of DEP is influenced by additive genetic effects11, and several previous studies have documented a considerable genetic overlap between DEP and multiple psychiatric as well as somatic disorders and traits12–17. Despite the substantial heritability of DEP and other psychiatric disorders, the potential for translating genetic insights into precision psychiatry has yet to be fulfilled, including demonstrating clinical utility of polygenic risk scores (PRSs)18–20.

Major advancement in understanding the genetic architecture of DEP has been achieved primarily via genome-wide association studies (GWAS) led by the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium (PGC). In 2021, GWAS results from the $\mathsf { P G C } ^ { 1 2 }$ , UK Biobank (UKB)14,21, FinnGen22 and 23andMe, Inc.23 were combined with data from the Million Veteran Program (MVP) in a large meta-analysis24, identifying 178 risk loci. The identified loci explain a small fraction of the overall heritability of DEP12,21,24 and even larger GWASs are needed to further elucidate genetic factors contributing to the risk of developing DEP and advance genetically informed patient stratification and outcome prediction toward clinical utility.

Here, we present a large GWAS meta-analysis of DEP, which included expanded iPSYCH25 and FinnGen22 cohorts, as well as PGC, UKB, 23andMe and MVP data24, revealing numerous new risk loci and infer pathophysiological implications of associated variants by intersecting with functional genomics data. We refine the genetic architecture of DEP and case subgroups and demonstrate the impact of DEP genetic risk on domains of cognitive performance. Leveraging nationwide longitudinal health data on the Danish iPSYCH cohort, we dissect the genetic architecture of single-episode and recurrent DEP as well as individuals who have developed ANX, BP, SZ and SUD comorbidities. Furthermore, to inform precision psychiatry approaches, we calculate time-dependent absolute risks and hazard rate ratios (HRRs) for developing recurrent DEP, ANX, BP, SZ and SUD depending on different polygenic burdens.

# Results

# Genome-wide association

We analyzed data from the large population-based case-cohort of $\mathbf { i } \mathsf { P S Y C H } ^ { 2 5 , 2 6 }$ , which includes genotypes from all individuals born in Denmark between 1981 and 2008 who have received treatment for DEP in hospitals and outpatient clinics (ICD-10 codes F32–F33 in the Danish Psychiatric Central Research Register27). Compared to the latest GWAS of DEP (from May 2021)24, which included 18,629 cases and 17,841 controls from the initial iPSYCH2012 cohort12,26, data from 11,710 individuals with DEP and 18,410 controls were included from the expanded iPSYCH2015 cohort25,26, which leads to a total of 30,618 individuals with DEP and 38,200 controls after relatedness pruning and removal of ancestry outliers. In addition, we included an updated dataset consisting of 28,098 individuals with DEP and 228,817 controls from the FinnGen22 study. When combining these with data from previously published samples from the PGC, UKB, 23andMe and MVP, the number of samples added up to a total of 371,184 individuals with DEP and 978,703 controls (Supplementary Table 1).

We performed a variance-weighted fixed-effects meta-analysis using METAL28, testing the effects of 6,037,120 single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) common across all datasets. This revealed a total of 303 genome-wide significant linkage disequilibrium (LD)-independent $( r ^ { 2 } < 0 . 1 )$ lead variants located in 243 distinct loci. A conditional association analysis using GCTA-COJO29,30 retained 251 independent SNPs in the 243 loci. Manhattan plots are shown in Fig. 1a,b, regional plots are provided in Supplementary Fig. 1 (X chromosome in Supplementary Fig. 2) (Supplementary Figs. 1–15 and 24–28 are available at https://doi. org/10.6084/m9.figshare.22139849) and details on lead variants are provided in Supplementary Table 2a, while GCTA-COJO independent variants are listed in Supplementary Table 2b. No statistically significant heterogeneity was observed between the datasets (Supplementary Fig. 3). A total of 64 of the 243 loci were new (not overlapping with the two most recent DEP meta-analyses)14,24 (Supplementary Fig. 4 and Supplementary Table 2a). The three most significant loci were located near NEGR1, in SORCS3 and in the HIST1 histone cluster, respectively. Among the new loci, the three strongest associations were in BPTF, LINGO1 and GRIA1 (Table 1). All three genes have been associated with monogenic forms of neurodevelopmental disorders31–33 and GRIA1, encoding glutamate ionotropic receptor AMPA type subunit 1 (GluA1), implicates a role of AMPA receptors in the etiology of DEP. We also note that the seventh-strongest new locus is located in GABRA1, which suggests a role of GABA receptors in developing DEP. Both AMPA and GABA receptors are targets for antidepressants34,35.

# Genetic correlations and heritability

Analyzing the GWAS meta-analysis by LD score regression (LDsc)36 produced a genomic inflation factor $( \lambda _ { G C } )$ estimate of 1.89 with an intercept of 1.06 (s.e. 0.01) and an attenuation ratio of 0.047 (s.e. 0.012) (Supplementary Table 3), indicating that $9 5 \%$ of the observed inflation of the test statistics (Supplementary Fig. 5) is due to a polygenic signal rather than population structure. The individual GWASs (iPSYCH2015, Howard et al.14, Wray et al.12, Levey et al.24 and FinnGen22) showed significant pairwise genetic correlations, ranging from $r _ { \mathrm { G } } { = } 0 . 7 7$ to $r _ { \mathrm { G } } = 0 . 9 5$ (Supplementary Table 4 and Supplementary Fig. 6), thus supporting that the GWAS results could be combined in a meta-analysis. We note that the SNP-heritability estimate36 for the iPSYCH cohort $( h _ { \mathrm { S N P } } ^ { 2 } = 0 . 1 6 7 ,$ , s.e. 0.014, prevalence ${ \bf \tau } = 0 . 2$ SNP) was significantly higher (range of $h _ { \mathrm { { S N P } } } ^ { 2 }$ difference, 0.057–0.098) compared to the other cohorts (Supplementary Table 3 and Supplementary Fig. 7). This may reflect that the sample from the iPSYCH cohort is more homogeneous and includes a relatively young population, with early onset and individuals with severe DEP who have been treated in hospitals37.

To dissect the genetic overlap observations further, we used uniand bivariate Gaussian mixture modeling, as implemented in MiXeR45, to quantify the actual number of variants that (1) explain $90 \%$ of the SNP heritability of DEP, and (2) overlap between DEP and DEP-correlated phenotypes. For comparison, we also included two phenotypes showing low genetic correlations with DEP (height and Alzheimer’s (ALZ) disease) and one with moderate correlation (epilepsy). Overall, 11,750 (s.e. 310) common variants were estimated to confer liability to DEP, suggesting that DEP is the most polygenic of the major psychiatric disorders evaluated, showing number of risk variants ranging between 7,000–10,500 (refs. 45,46) (Fig. 2, Supplementary Fig. 9-1 and Supplementary Table 6a). MiXeR considers all variants irrespective of the direction of correlation (both variants with same and opposite direction of effects, hereafter collectively referred to as ‘influencing’ variants). The vast majority of variants conferring risk to the other psychiatric disorders investigated were found to influence DEP (range $8 7 - 9 9 \%$ ; Fig. 2 and Supplementary Table 6a), most pronounced for ANX, SZ, BP and ADHD46 with $9 5 { - } 9 9 \%$ of their risk variants also influencing DEP. The other correlated traits also showed substantial overlap with DEP (range $8 7 - 9 7 \%$ ), whereas height and ALZ disease did not. We note that nearly all $( 9 9 \% )$ of the DEP risk variants were found to influence educational attainment. The fraction of variants affecting both traits in the same (concordant) direction varied considerably; lowest for educational attainment $( 4 2 \% )$ and highest for SUD $( 8 6 \% )$ and ANX $(89 \% )$ .

Local genetic correlations in 2,495 loci analyzed using LAVA47 (Supplementary Fig. 9-2 and Supplementary Table 6b) generally supported the results of the MiXeR analyses. The proportion of loci with positive genetic correlations was significantly correlated with the proportion of MiXeR-estimated influencing variants with concordant effects (Pearson’s correlation $r = 0 . 7 6$ , $P { = } 0 . 0 0 6 9$ ; Supplementary Fig. 9-3). Although LAVA estimates generally seem to be more extreme than MiXeR, these findings support the validity of MiXeR estimates of mixed-effect directions (Supplementary Fig. 9-1 and Supplementary Table 6a).

# Transcriptome-wide association and GWAS-eQTL prioritization

To identify and prioritize putative causal genes, we performed a transcriptome-wide association study (TWAS), imputing the genetically regulated gene expression using EpiXcan49 and models trained on expression data from the PsychENCODE Consortium50,51 for genes and isoforms detected in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC). Among 34,646 transcripts (genes and isoforms) tested, we identified 2,541 transcripts at $\mathsf { F D R } < 0 . 0 5$ and, after Bonferroni correction of all transcripts tested, 324 transcripts from 201 genes showed significant differential imputed gene expression (Bonferroni $P$ value threshold, $P { = } 1 . 4 4 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 0 } ,$ ) in DLPFC between DEP and control groups (Supplementary Table 9). The Bonferroni-significant transcripts were located in 88 independent regions52. The top gene/isoform is labeled in Fig. 1d and regional TWAS/GWAS Miami plots for each of the 88 regions are shown in Supplementary Fig. 10. Six highlights are shown in Extended Data Fig. 1. In 38 of the 88 regions, the top transcript was $_ { > 1 0 0 }$ times more significant than the second-most-associated gene/isoform in the region (Supplementary Table 9b), appointing those as plausible causal candidates.

To further prioritize likely causal genes and variants, we performed colocalization analyses integrating fine-mapped GWAS results, using the CAUSALdb pipeline (Methods) and expression quantitative trait loci (eQTL) data from a meta-analysis of three brain datasets53 applying a fixed-effects model. First, we adopted the Coloc method54,55, which revealed 13 genes with strong evidence for both GWAS association, eQTL association and colocalization (with a posterior probability of $\mathsf { P P H 4 } > 0 . 8$ ; Supplementary Table 10). The three top-ranked genes were: FURIN, NEGR1 and CKS2. Second, we conducted the eQTL and GWAS causal variants identification in associated regions (eCAVIAR) approach56, in which both eQTL and GWAS were fine-mapped and the product of posterior probability (CLPP) was calculated, prioritizing variants with at least a single variant with $\mathrm { C L P P } \ge 0 . 0 1$ . The eCAVIAR approach revealed five prioritized variants located in three genes: FURIN, GPR27 and TCTA (Supplementary Table 11 and Supplementary Fig. 11).

# Tissue and cell-type enrichment

We next tested whether the GWAS results were enriched with respect to the transcriptomic profiles of human tissues. At the specific tissue level, we found significant enrichment exclusively in brain tissues, including all the brain tissues analyzed (Bonferroni-corrected P value threshold $P { = } 0 . 0 0 0 9 3$ , with $P$ values ranging from $P { = } 1 . 1 7 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 }$ to $P { = } 8 . 3 6 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 3 }$ for the brain tissues; Supplementary Fig. 12). Cell-type enrichment analyses revealed experiment-wide significant association (across all 13 datasets tested) of primarily neuronal cell types, including dopaminergic and GABAergic neurons (P values ranging from $P { = } 1 . 3 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 } \mathrm { t o } P { = } 5 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 1 }$ for the significant associations after Bonferroni correction $P { = } 1 . 8 7 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 }$ ; Fig. 3a and Supplementary Fig. 13). Both GABAergic neurons and oligodendrocyte progenitor cells (OPCs) of the human prefrontal cortex were already enriched at prenatal stages.

To further evaluate cell-type enrichment, we intersected the GWAS results with two recent epigenomic maps of cell-specific open chroma$\mathrm { t i n } ^ { 5 7 , 5 8 }$ using an LD score partitioned heritability approach59. Again, we observed a clear contrast between the enrichments in the brain and non-brain tissue (Fig. 3b). Conversely, the enrichments of genetic DEP risk in astrocytes and oligodendrocyte lineages have not been reported previously, albeit having support in behavioral and postmortem studies62–64.

# Association with cognitive performance

Educational attainment was among the traits showing strong negative genetic correlation with DEP (Supplementary Table 5a). To further evaluate the impact of DEP genetic risk on cognition, we analyzed the association of DEP polygenic scores with 15 cognitive measures in the Philadelphia Neurodevelopmental Cohort (PN $\scriptstyle 1 \mathbf { C } ; n = 4 , 9 7 3 ) ^ { 6 5 , 6 6 }$ . Cognitive performance was measured by the Computerized Neurocognitive Battery $\textstyle ( \mathbf { C N B } ) ^ { 6 7 }$ , including 14 tests in five domains: executive control, episodic memory, complex cognitive processing, social cognition and sensorimotor speed. In addition, the wide range achievement test (WRAT-4)68 was used as a proxy measure for overall $1 \mathrm { Q } ^ { 6 6 }$ . The DEP PRS was negatively associated with abstraction and mental flexibility $( \beta = - 0 . 0 3 9$ , s.e. 0.014, $\mathsf { F D R } = 0 . 0 3 0$ ; Fig. 4 and Supplementary Table 12a). Applying a narrower definition of DEP (Methods, Supplementary Table 13 and Supplementary Fig. 14) yielded moderately stronger negative associations, with attention, abstraction and mental flexibility in the executive control domain and verbal reasoning in the reasoning domain, surpassing statistical significance (Fig. 4 and Supplementary Table 12b). These results are consistent with observational studies reporting that individuals with DEP display lower performance in cognitive domains such as executive function, memory, language and attention69–71, and demonstrate that genetic DEP risk is associated with attenuated functioning in specific cognitive domains in a community cohort of youths (aged 8–21 years at enrollment).

# Polygenic architecture and comorbidity rates for recurrency

To dissect the polygenic architecture of single-episode and recurrent DEP, we used dates of diagnosis in the Danish Psychiatric Central Research Register to group the total of 30,618 individuals with DEP from the iPSYCH cohort into single-episode $\scriptstyle n = 2 4 , 1 0 1 )$ ) and recurrent DEP $( n = 6 , 5 1 7 )$ ) case groups (Methods and Supplementary Table 14 provides details). We conducted three GWASs: (1) single-episode versus control groups; (2) recurrent DEP versus control groups; and (3) recurrent versus single-episode DEP groups (excluding all controls). SNP-heritability estimates were similar for recurrent and single-episode DEP and, for the case-only analysis, not significantly different from zero (Supplementary Table 3 and Supplementary Fig. 7). Likewise, the genetic correlations with other phenotypes showed similar patterns for single-episode and recurrent DEP (Supplementary Fig. 15 and Supplementary Table 15).

To further dissect the genetic architecture of the two subgroups, we investigated the PRS load for psychiatric disorders and traits showing strong genetic correlation with DEP including ANX, BP, SZ, ASD, ADHD, SUD, substance use (SU) and neuroticism (Supplementary Fig. 16-1 and Supplementary Table 16a). Prompted by the results of the mvPRS analysis, we performed the same analysis for PRSs from other psychiatric disorders, which yielded significant results only for BP PRS $\mathrm { ( H R R _ { 1 0 / m i d d l e } = 1 . 1 0 }$ , s.e. 0.04, ${ P = 0 . 0 2 1 }$ ; Supplementary Fig. 17-1 and Supplementary Table 17a). # Risk prediction of comorbid psychiatric disorders

To assess the utility of PRS in prediction of developing comorbid disorders, we performed Cox’s regression analysis on the DEP case group with and without additional diagnosis of ANX, BP, SZ and/or SUD. In addition to PRSs for the individual disorders, we included four aggregate scores, one for each disorder, combining all PRSs into a single score weighted by their association with ANX, BP, SZ and SUD, respectively (Methods).

For all four co-diagnoses, we found that the HRR increased with higher polygenic loads and was statistically significant for the tenth PRS decile compared to the middle deciles (Fig. 5, Extended

Data Figs. 6–9 and Supplementary Table 19–22). This was the case for both the single-disorder PRSs and (more strongly) for the aggregate (SUM) PRSs. Similarly, the absolute risk trajectories showed significant differences across deciles for all co-diagnoses, most considerably for BP and SUD with absolute risks for the tenth aggregate PRS decile reaching $1 3 \%$ and $21 \%$ , respectively, representing a 3.25 (BP) and 5.25 (SUD) fold increase in risk compared to the least burdened (first decile) case groups.

Performing sex-specific analyses revealed substantial differences in absolute risks between the sexes (Supplementary Figs. 20–23), most considerably for developing SUD and SZ. For SUD, male risks reached $3 5 \%$ , $1 5 \%$ and $6 \%$ for PRS decile 10, middle and 1, respectively, whereas female risks were $1 5 \%$ , $7 \%$ and $3 \%$ . For SZ, the risks for males were $2 5 \%$ , $1 3 \%$ and $1 3 \%$ compared to $9 \%$ , $8 \%$ and $8 \%$ for females.

The overall most differentiating absolute risks for developing a comorbid disorder were seen for males in the top aggregate PRS decile, reaching risks of $3 5 \%$ and $2 5 \%$ for developing SUD and SZ, respectively, in contrast to the lowest decile case groups (both sexes) with risks of $4 \%$ (SUD) and $8 \%$ (SZ) and the background population without a DEP diagnosis with risks of $2 \%$ (SUD) and $1 \%$ (SZ) for the middle deciles.

In comparison to the background population without any DEP diagnoses, the absolute risks for the top decile case groups were between 7- and 30-times higher. Particularly for SUD risk, the top decile of the background population surpassed the risk of the lowest decile case group (Fig. 5J).

# Discussion

We performed a DEP GWAS meta-analysis of more than 1.3 million individuals, identifying 251 independent risk variants in 243 genomic loci, of which 64 are new. We prioritized likely causal genes and revealed new enrichments of neurodevelopmental and functional pathways, prenatal GABAergic neurons, astrocytes and oligodendrocyte lineages.

Dissecting the genetic and clinical heterogeneity, we identified distinct polygenic architectures across subgroups of DEP and demonstrated increased, sex-dependent, risks for recurrence and psychiatric comorbidity among DEP cases with the highest polygenic burden, informing precision psychiatry approaches.

Among the new loci, we highlight GRIA1 and GABRA1, encoding a glutamate ionotropic AMPA type 1 receptor subunit (GluA1) and a GABA receptor subunit (α1), respectively (Supplementary Table 2). The two genes also showed significant imputed differential expression in the DLPFC (Supplementary Table 9) in our TWAS76–78. Our pathway analysis reinforced previous reports12,14,24 and extended the enrichment of glutamatergic and GABAergic synapses and functions, further indicating that glutamatergic and GABAergic dysfunctions are key etiologic components in DEP. Along with the observed enrichment of GABAergic cell types already present during the prenatal stage, this supports the accumulating multidisciplinary evidence that implicate excitatory/ inhibitory imbalance with DEP79 and other psychiatric disorders80–83.

Our TWAS found that GRIA1 expression in DLPFC was decreased in the DEP case group compared to control. This is consistent with findings in preclinical pharmacological studies of mood disorders showing promising results of AMPA receptor potentiators35, as well as the convergent rapid and sustained increase in GluA1 and other synaptic proteins associated with most fast-acting antidepressants34. Thus, our results provide human genetic evidence that emphasizes positive modulation of the AMPA receptor as a potential pharmacological approach.

In contrast to a reported decrease in GABRA1 expression in the DLPFC in individuals with suicidal DEP84,85, our TWAS pointed to an increased expression in individuals with DEP. $\mathbf { G A B A _ { A } }$ receptors provide critical inhibitory control of the firing of glutamatergic excitatory neurons and they are the binding partner of several drugs in mood disorders and potential drug targets for other psychiatric disorders86–88. Antidepressant effects have been associated with pharmacological modulation of both synaptic89 and extra-synaptic $\mathbf { G A B A _ { A } }$ receptors90 and our findings support the further study of pharmacological modulators of these receptors in the treatment of DEP.

We also highlight convergent lines of evidence for a neurodevelopmental component in DEP, emanating from (1) new loci (for example the top three new loci located in genes associated with Mendelian neurodevelopmental disorders); (2) pathway analyses showing enrichment of gene sets related to neuronal development; (3) enrichment of prenatal cell types; (4) association of DEP genetic risk with attenuated cognitive performance in a community youth cohort; and (5) MiXeR analyses showing substantial genetic overlap with ASD, ADHD and SZ. Although partly overlapping, these results implicate a role of neurodevelopmental processes in the etiology of DEP.

The MiXeR bivariate Gaussian mixture modeling revealed compelling genetic overlap between DEP and other traits when considering both concordant and discordant variants, suggesting that psychiatric disorders and correlated traits are substantially more intertwined than indicated by their genetic correlations. The most drastic results were observed for educational attainment, showing that $9 9 \%$ of the DEP risk variants are also influencing educational attainment and, vice versa, $8 8 \%$ of educational attainment variants are influencing DEP. This almost complete overlap in influencing variants, with a majority of discordant variants $(5 8 \% )$ , is consistent with overall negative genetic correlation between the two traits $( r _ { \mathrm { G } } = - 0 . 2 3 )$ and refines the understanding of their polygenic architecture appreciably. Although generally less pronounced, a similar picture was observed for the other mental health and behavioral traits examined. These results corroborate and extend previous results on partly older and smaller GWASs91. A combination of (altogether) only around 10,000–15,000 variants seem to explain $90 \%$ of the SNP heritability of the investigated major psychiatric disorders and correlated behavioral and cognitive traits. This narrows the search space for risk variants to around $1 \%$ of the $1 0 ^ { 6 }$ quasi-independent SNPs genome-wide and indicates that it is mainly the size and directions of the SNP effects, rather than different SNPs/loci, that influence the development of a specific psychiatric disorder. Furthermore, the considerable overlap suggests that future fine-mapping efforts to pinpoint the causal variants could increase power by combining data from traits with overlapping influencing variants when accounting for the directional effects.

In the Danish iPSYCH cohort, we assessed PRS-based predictions for developing recurrent DEP and psychiatric comorbidity over time and demonstrated statistically significant differences in hazard rates and absolute risks between individuals with DEP in the highest PRS deciles compared to other deciles for all the outcomes (developing recurrence, ANX, BP, SZ or SUD).

The iPSYCH cohort includes relatively young individuals (mean age 23.37 years, s.e.m. 6.9, at follow-up on 31 December 2016) and thus, a substantial number of those with DEP will develop recurrent DEP and/ or comorbidities later in life. This indicates that observed risks, and likely also the differences across PRS deciles, will increase further over time. Moreover, risk prediction could probably be improved by including other risk factors such as for example family history92 and clinical/ phenotypic variables, as has been shown in other complex disorders93.

Our findings have potential clinically relevant applications. For instance, in clinical settings, a targeted effort could be envisaged that offers more frequent monitoring for development of for example BP, SZ or ANX among individuals with DEP who have the highest PRS (or, preferably, combined genetic and clinical risk) to obtain early diagnosis and initiate early treatment, which may have beneficial effects94,95. Similarly, identifying individuals with DEP at high genetic risk for developing SUD could be an actionable and informative point of attention for both the physician and the patient and, with focused intervention measures96, possibly preventing the subsequent development of SUD (the primary prevention of secondary comorbidities97). Validation in clinical settings of such potential applications is warranted in the future18,98.

Our study has several limitations. Our analyses focused on individuals with European ancestry to avoid confounding from population substructure and admixture. We performed several analyses to assess the generalizability to individuals of diverse and admixed genetic ancestry but much larger samples are needed to address this important aspect. Another basic limitation is that still most of the risk loci remain to be discovered and scrutinized to inform on the pathophysiology. Additionally, studies in clinical settings are needed to assess the clinical utility of our findings.

In conclusion, we have advanced the understanding of the genetic architecture of DEP, the genetically driven pathophysiology and the prediction of pertinent outcomes that should be informative for future precision psychiatry approaches in DEP.

# Methods

# Samples

The iPSYCH2015 sample is a nationwide population sample extracted from a baseline cohort consisting of all children born in Denmark between 1 May 1981 and 31 December 2008, who were alive and resided in Denmark on their 1-year birthday, and who have a known mother25,26. Individuals diagnosed with one (or more) of six major psychiatric disorders (ADHD, ASD, DEP, BP, SZ and anorexia) were identified via the Danish Psychiatric Central Research Register27, which includes data on all individuals treated in Denmark at psychiatric hospitals (from 1969 onward) as well as at outpatient psychiatric clinics (from 1995 onward). A random sample from the same birth cohort was chosen as a control group. The iPSYCH2015 cohort consists of the initial case-cohort sample iPSYCH2012 (ref. 26) and the recent extension iPSYCH2015i (ref. 25).

Individuals with an ICD-10 F32–F33 diagnosis in 2016 or earlier were considered as DEP cases and the random population-based sample excluding individuals with a DEP diagnosis were used as controls. All individuals diagnosed with BP were excluded, except for the analyses involving co-occurrence of DEP and BP diagnosis. Individuals with DEP were divided into two groups: (1) those diagnosed with a single episode of DEP and (2) those fulfilling the criteria for recurrent DEP. The date of the first DEP episode for each individual was defined as the start date of the first contact in the Danish Psychiatric Central Research Register with an ICD-10 code of F32–F33 diagnoses at an age of 10 years or older. When defining later DEP episodes, only contacts with the ICD-10 codes F32, F33.0–F33.3 and F33.8–F33.9 were considered. Individuals with the ICD-10 code F33.4 ‘major depressive disorder, recurrent, in remission’ were omitted because these contacts are unlikely to indicate a new episode of DEP. Only ICD-10 codes F32, F33.0–F33.3 and F33.8–F33.9 diagnoses at dates later than 60 days after the end date for all previous ICD-10 F32–F33 diagnoses were considered as new episodes of DEP, thus categorizing that individual as having recurrent DEP.

In addition to DEP phenotypes, individuals with the following diagnoses were recorded in the iPSYCH sample: BP (F30–F31), SZ (F20), SUD (F10–F19, excluding F1X.0 acute intoxication), ANX (F40–F43), ASD (F84.0, F84.1, F84.5, F84.8 or F84) and ADHD (F90.0).

In the iPSYCH2015 cohort25, we added 11,710 individuals with DEP and 18,410 controls to the iPSYCH2012 sample12,26, summarizing to a total of 34,095 cases and 45,393 controls before relatedness pruning and removal of ancestry outliers (Supplementary Table 14). A total of 30,618 cases and 38,200 controls were retained after relatedness pruning and removal of ancestry outliers. Of these, 6,517 had recurrent episodes of DEP (see definition above), whereas 24,101 were classified as having single episodes of DEP.

The number of individuals with an additional psychiatric diagnosis (BP, SZ, ANX, ASD, ADHD and SUD) are shown in Extended Data Table 1. When comparing these frequencies with published estimates of lifetime prevalence three main factors are important to keep in mind: (1) the investigated Danish iPSYCH cohort is young (individuals born between 1981 and 2008; mean age 23.37 years at follow-up); (2) phenotypes are based on register-based hospital-diagnoses; and (3) people with early onset of affective disorders have a greater absolute risk of developing SZ and related disorders, compared to those with later onsets9 . Lifetime risk of hospital-diagnosed SZ and DEP (ICD-10 F32–F33) in the Danish population has been estimated to $1 . 7 5 \%$ (average of $1 . 9 3 \%$ in males and $1 . 5 6 \%$ in females) and $1 2 . 3 \%$ (average of $9 . 0 7 \%$ in males and $1 5 . 5 \%$ in females), respectively3 . Extended Data Table 1 shows that the frequencies of SZ and DEP in the young, randomly selected iPSYCH-population sample are $1 \%$ and $2 . 7 \%$ , respectively, based on register-based hospital diagnosis. SZ has a relatively early onset, whereas DEP has a relatively late onset, hence the relatively low observed $2 . 7 \%$ frequency of DEP in the random population-based sample of iPSYCH. Thus, the young iPSYCH sample is likely to capture relatively severe cases of early-onset DEP with a high risk for developing ${ \ S Z ^ { 9 } }$ . These factors explain the apparently high frequency of cases of DEP with a SZ co-diagnosis observed in the iPSYCH sample. As the iPSYCH cohort grows older, and more individuals develop DEP, we will expect the frequency of SZ co-diagnosis among DEP cases to be reduced.

Whether recurrent DEP was associated with increased comorbidity compared to single-episode DEP was tested using logistic regression for each additional diagnosis while adjusting for age. Individuals were grouped in 5-year age bins to construct dummy variables for the age adjustment (Extended Data Fig. 2).

All analyses of the iPSYCH sample were performed at the secured national GenomeDK high-performance computing cluster in Denmark.

# Genotyping, quality control and imputation

iPSYCH2015 samples were linked via the unique national personal identification number to the Danish Neonatal Screening Biobank at Statens Serum Institute, where DNA was extracted from Guthrie cards, and whole-genome amplification was performed in triplicate, as described previously99,100.

Genotyping in iPSYCH2012 was performed using the PsychArray v.1.0 (Illumina), while genotyping of iPSYCH2015i was conducted using the Global Screening Array v.2 (Illumina). As the two samples were genotyped on different platforms, they were quality controlled and imputed separately.

Genotyping of the iPSYCH2012 sample was performed at the Broad Institute of Harvard and MIT with PsychChip arrays from Illumina according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Genotype calling of markers with minor allele frequency (MAF) $\bf \Pi \beta | \geq 0 . 0 1$ was performed by merging call sets from GenCall v.1.6.2.2 (ref. 101) and Birdseed 1.6 (ref. 102) and less frequent variants $( \mathsf { M A F } < 0 . 0 1 $ ) were called with zCall 1 (ref. 103). Genotyping and data processing were carried out in 23 waves. Genotyping of the iPSYCH2015i sample was performed at the Statens Serum Institute using the Global Screening Array v.2 with a multi-disease drop-in (Illumina) according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Genotype calling of markers was performed using GenTrain v.3. Pre-imputation quality control was performed separately for each genotyping wave using the Ricopili104 pipeline with the specified parameters in the following order. Initially SNPs with a call rate $< 0 . 9 5$ were removed and subsequently all individuals with a call rate in cases or controls of $\mathbf { \omega } _ { < 0 . 9 5 }$ or an autosomal heterozygosity deviation $\boldsymbol { F } _ { \mathrm { H E T } }$ outside the interval $( - 0 . 2 ; 0 . 2 )$ were removed. Individuals where stated sex was not consistent with sex derived from genotypes were flagged. Subsequently quality control was conducted at the marker level, keeping markers with a call rate $_ { 2 0 . 9 8 }$ , missing difference ${ \le } 0 . 0 2$ between cases and controls, with MAF $\geq 0 . 0 1$ , Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium in controls $P { \ge } 1 \times 1 0 ^ { - 6 }$ and Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium in cases $P { \geq } 1 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 0 }$ (https://sites.google.com/a/broadinstitute.org/ricopili/ preimputation-qc provides further details).

The effects of three batch variables on marker genotypes were tested in iPSYCH2012 (ArrayPlate.ID, PreProc.Plate and wave) and iPSYCH2015i separately (Array.Batch, ArrayPlate.ID and PreProc.Plate). This was conducted using a relatedness-pruned dataset with ancestry outliers removed to avoid removal of markers where batch effects were caused by population structure or cryptic relatedness rather than genotype artifacts.

Pairwise relatedness coefficients $\mathbf { \Pi } ( { \hat { \pi } } )$ were estimated with PLINK using an LD-pruned and MAF-filtered set of SNPs (SNPs only, window $\mathsf { s i z e } = 5 , 0 0 0$ , step size $= 3 0 0$ , $r ^ { 2 } < 0 . 0 5$ , $\mathbf { M A F } > 0 . 0 5$ ). Principalcomponent analysis (PCA) was conducted using the same set of LD-pruned and MAF-filtered SNPs, with random removal of one member of each pair with a relatedness coefficient $\mathbf { \rho } ( \hat { \pi } ) > 0 . 2$ . Eigenvectors were inferred using EIGENSOFT v.6.1.4 (ref. 105) on the relatednesspruned set of individuals and subsequently projecting all individuals onto those eigenvectors based on their genotypes. Individuals with all four grandparents born in Denmark were used as a reference for constructing a three-dimensional ellipsoid using principal components (PCs) 1, 2 and 3 with a radius of $5 \times { \bf s . d }$ . from the mean. Individuals located outside this ellipsoid were removed before testing for batch effects. For each of the batch variables, each genotyped marker was tested for association with each batch versus the remaining batches pooled, using PLINK v.1.90b4.

The exclusion of SNPs strongly associated with any of the batch variables were based on their minimum P value across all associations per variable. The cutoff for the wave and Array.Batch was m $\mathsf { u i n } ( P ) < 2 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 0 }$ and for PreProc.Plate and ArrayPlate.ID $\operatorname* { m i n } ( P ) < 2 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 2 }$ , based on a Bonferroni correction for the number of markers tested and the number of associations performed per batch variable taking into account that batch variables are nested.

After removing SNPs falling for any of the above cutoff the remaining distribution was evaluated using QQ-plots. The expected minimum P distribution was calculated using the inverse cumulative distribution of $n$ independent distributions as suggested in the supplementary information of Schork et al.106; $n$ being the number batch-variable values.

iPSYCH2012 and iPSYCH2015i were imputed separately using the Ricopili pipeline104. All 23 genotyping waves of iPSYCH2012 were imputed together. Prephasing was conducted using Eagle v.2.3.5 (ref. 107) and the subsequent imputation was conducted using Minimac3 (ref. 108), using the downloadable version of the Haplotype Reference Consortium (HRC) (accession no. EGAD00001002729)109 as a reference.

# Relatedness pruning and removal of ancestry outliers

Best-guess genotypes from iPSYCH2012 and iPSYCH2015i were merged, filtered and LD-pruned down to a set of roughly 30,000 markers, with imputation INFO score ${ \bf \ } > 0 . 8$ , $r ^ { 2 } < 0 . 0 7 5$ , located outside regions of long-range LD as defined by Price et al.110, $\mathbf { M A F } > 0 . 0 5$ and no deviation from Hardy–Weinberg proportions $( P { > } 1 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 } )$ . Relatedness coefficients, based on ‘identity-by-state’, were estimated using PLINK v.1.9, to identify related (and duplicated samples), with $\hbar > 0 . 2$ and removing related individuals at random, but preferring cases over controls. PCA was carried out using the same set of filtered and LD-pruned SNPs as implemented in the Ricopili pipeline104. A subsample of European ancestry was selected as an ellipsoid in the space of PC1–3 centered and scaled using the mean and ${ \boldsymbol { 8 \times } }$ s.d. of the subsample whose grandparents were all known to be born in Denmark.

All the analyses described below were conducted using the relatedness and ancestry-pruned dataset, but some analyses were in addition also performed among ancestry outliers (see below, Supplementary Figs. 24–28 and Supplementary Tables 23–28) (Supplementary Figs. 1–15 and 24–28 are available at https://doi.org/10.6084/ m9.figshare.22139849). A second PCA was performed on the sample, excluding ancestry outliers, using EIGENSOFT105 as implemented in the Ricopili pipeline104, which was used for adjusting for any remaining population substructure in downstream analyses.

# FinnGen

The FinnGen22 (https://www.finngen.fi/en) study combines genotype data with longitudinal health register data of Finland, including the causes of death, inpatient, outpatient and drug reimbursement registers. Individuals were genotyped with Illumina and Affymetrix arrays (Illumina and Thermo Fisher Scientific) as described (https://www. finngen.fi/en/researchers/genotyping). Genotyping and imputation with the Finnish population-specific SISu v.3 reference panel were conducted, as described (https://www.protocols.io/view/genotypeimputation-workflow-v3-0-xbgfijw). SNPs were pruned for $\mathbf { M A F } \geq 0 . 0 1$ and imputation info score ${ \bf > 0 . 6 . }$ GWAS was performed using the Scalable and Accurate Implementation of GEneralized mixed model (SAIGE) v.0.20 (ref. 111) with a kinship matrix as a random effect and age, sex, the first ten PCs and genotyping batch as fixed effects.

A GWAS for DEP was conducted in FinnGen22 release 6 samples containing 28,098 cases (ICD-10/ICD-9, F32 or F33) and 228,817 controls without manic episodes (ICD-10, F30), bipolar affective disorder (ICD10, F31; ICD-9, 296(2–7); ICD-8, 296), persistent mood disorders (ICD-10, F34) or other or unspecified mood disorder (ICD-10, F38, F39; ICD-8, 29699). Mood (affective) disorders (ICD-10, F30–F39) from FinnGen release 2 was included in the latest DEP GWAS meta-analysis by Levey et al.24, but the present study includes DEP cases (ICD-10, F32–F33) from FinnGen release 6 for the first time.

A GWAS for ANX disorders was conducted in the FinnGen22 release 6 sample, consisting of 7,671 cases with a generalized ANX disorder (ICD-10, F41.1; ICD-9, 3000C; ICD-8, 300,00), panic disorder (ICD-10, F41.0; ICD-9, 3000B, 3002B) or phobic ANX (ICD-10, F40; ICD-9, 3002C, 3002D, 3002X) diagnoses and 161,438 controls without a history of any psychiatric diagnoses (ICD-10, F00–F99; ICD-9, 290–319; ICD-8, 290–315). With ICD-9 codes the first three digits signify the numerical value (for example, 3002B is included in the code range 290–319). Individuals with psychotic disorders (ICD-10, F2; ICD-9, 295, 297, 298; ICD-8, 295, 297, 298), ASD (ICD-10, F84.0, F84.1, F84.5; ICD-9, 2990; ICD-8, 299,99) or intellectual disability (ICD-10, F7; ICD-9, 317–319; ICD-8, 311–315) were excluded. Additionally, the age range of controls was adjusted to match to that of cases and a more stringent filtering on imputation info score was used for the ANX GWAS $( \mathsf { I N F O } > 0 . 8 )$ . This GWAS of core ANX in the FinnGen22 cohort was meta-analyzed with a GWAS of self-report of physician diagnosis of ANX in the $\mathsf { M V P } ^ { \mathrm { 1 1 2 } }$ , with the purpose of generating weights for an ANX PRS (see below).

# Statistical analysis

GWAS analyses. GWAS within iPSYCH were conducted using the Ricopili pipline104, applying a logistic regression model using dosages of the imputed genotypes. Analyses were adjusted for PC1–10 from the second PCA using the remaining subsample after removal of ancestry outliers and pruning for relatedness. In addition, individuals with a diagnosis of BP were excluded.

Summary statistics from iPSYCH2015 and the following external samples were included in a fixed-effects variance-weighted meta-analysis in METAL28: (1) 230,118 broadly defined DEP cases and 545,339 controls from Howard et al.14, including self-reported DEP of 23andMe23 and the broadly defined DEP phenotype of UKB, 35,077 narrowly defined DEP cases and 95,406 controls from Wray et al.12, excluding 23andMe23; (2) DEP phenotypes from the MVP24 based on ICD codes derived from electronic health records (83,810 cases and 166,405 controls); and (3) 28,098 cases with ICD-10 F32 and/or F33 diagnoses and 228,817 controls from FinnGen22.

For comparison, we also conducted a GWAS and several downstream analyses using a narrower definition of DEP that excluded self-reported DEP of 23andMe12,23 and the broadly defined DEP phenotype of $\mathrm { \mathsf { U K B } } ^ { 1 4 , 2 1 }$ (Supplementary Fig. 14 and Supplementary Table 13). The results of the downstream analyses were very similar to the primary analyses without noteworthy differences, except for the DEP PRS analysis of cognitive performance in the PNC cohort, which is mentioned in Results.

SNP heritability, genetic correlations and overlap with other phenotypes. We estimated SNP heritability $( h _ { \mathrm { ~ { ~ \scriptsize ~ S N P } } } ^ { 2 } )$ for iPSYCH2015 cases with DEP, single-episode and recurrent DEP and for the external GWAS summary statistics outlined above using LD score regression36. Genetic correlations within iPSYCH and between iPSYCH and external GWAS summary statistics was estimated using LD score regression36.

In addition, the genetic correlations of DEP meta-analysis, single-episode and recurrent DEP with other phenotypes were, evaluated using LD score regression113 at the LD Hub38 website. We used all available phenotypes on the LD Hub, but we performed analyses for the UKB traits $\scriptstyle ( n = 5 9 7$ ) and the remaining individual phenotypes $( n = 2 5 8 )$ ) separately. To make our analyses more comparable with previous publications, and to avoid overly conservative correction of the many highly correlated traits, levels of experiment-wide significance (Bonferroni correction for number of tests applied) were also established separately within the two groups; in the UKB traits $( P < 8 . 3 8 \times 1 0 ^ { - 5 } )$ ) and the remaining individual phenotypes $( P { < } 0 . 0 0 0 1 9 )$ ), respectively. LD Hub traits were supplemented with LD score regression36 analyses performed locally using updated or in-house GWAS summary statistics for traits not available at LD Hub. These include ADHD, $\mathsf { A S D } ^ { 1 5 }$ , cannabis use disorder39, cannabis use114, alcohol dependence115, drinks per week, smoking ever116, age of initiation as a regular smoker, current versus a former smoker (smoking cessation), number of cigarettes per day and smoking initiation1 17

We applied MiXeR45 on our DEP GWAS summary statistics and a selection of additional traits (Supplementary Table 6a, Fig. 2 and Supplementary Fig. 9-1) to estimate (1) the number of variants explaining $90 \%$ of the SNP heritability of each trait and (2) the genetic overlap between DEP and each trait. The additional traits included in the analyses are $\mathsf { A D H D } ^ { 4 6 }$ , ANX (FinnGen $^ { 2 2 } +  { \mathsf { M V P } } ^ { 1 1 0 }$ ), $\mathbf { A S D } ^ { 1 5 }$ , BP118, educational attainment119, neuroticism120, $\mathsf { S } Z ^ { 1 2 1 }$ , smoking initiation117 and SUD39,115. For comparison we included non-psychiatric traits $( { \bf A } { \bf L } Z ^ { 1 2 2 }$ epilepsy123 and height124) not necessarily expected to show strong genetic correlations with DEP. MiXeR analyses were conducted with default settings (https://github.com/precimed/mixer) in a two-step process: (1) a univariate model for each trait to produce estimates of the proportion of variants with non-zero additive genetic effect on the trait (‘polygenicity’) and the variance of effect sizes of these non-zero variants (‘discoverability’) and (2) the variance estimates obtained in the univariate analysis were applied in the bivariate model (DEP versus each of the additional traits) to obtain four estimates representing (1) zero-effect SNPs in both traits; (2) SNPs with a specific non-zero effect on trait 1; (3) SNPs with a specific non-zero effect on trait 2; and (4) SNPs with a non-zero effect on both traits. Estimates of polygenic overlap and genetic correlation between pairs of traits were obtained by combining these four components.

As a complement to MiXeR, we estimated local genetic correlations $( r _ { \mathrm { G } } )$ of DEP and the additional traits listed above using LAVA47. LAVA estimates local $r _ { \mathrm G }$ across 2,495 semi-independent genetic loci of approximately equal size (\~1 Mb) in a two-step process: (1) univariate analysis, where the observed $h _ { \mathsf { \Phi } \mathrm { s N P } } ^ { 2 }$ is estimated for each locus for each trait and (2) bivariate analysis, where the local genetic covariance is estimated for each locus using the method of moments. Genetic loci included in the bivariate analysis were filtered according to their local $h _ { \mathrm { { \scriptsize ~ S N P } } } ^ { 2 }$ using a significance threshold of $P { < } 1 0 ^ { - 4 }$ , consistent with previous usage of LAVA47,91. Sample overlap was controlled using LD score regression36. Significance testing was performed using simulation-based $P$ values and we used the FDR to adjust for multiple testing, reporting loci with $\mathsf { F D R } < 0 . 0 5$ .

While the genetic correlation analysis provides a summary measure of the genome-wide correlation of SNP effect sizes, the bivariate MiXeR analysis estimates the number of overlapping SNPs/loci between the two traits regardless of the direction of effects (both concordant and discordant effects) and calculates the fraction of concordant variants. For instance, a genetic correlation of zero can be seen for traits that have no overlapping risk SNPs as well as for traits that have completely overlapping risk SNPs with $50 \%$ concordant and $50 \%$ discordant effect directions. So, as the huge overlap observed between DEP and educational attainment (Supplementary Table 6a and Supplementary Fig. 9-1) is based on a majority of discordant variants $( 5 8 \% )$ , and fewer concordant variants $( 4 2 \% )$ , it is consistent with the overall negative correlation observed between the two traits.

As an additional validation of MiXeR results, we tested the hypothesis that MiXeR- and LAVA-derived measures of mixed-effect directions were correlated. We calculated the Pearson correlation coefficient for (1) the proportion of shared ‘causal’ variants with concordant effects (causal variants affecting both traits in the same direction) estimated using MiXeR and (2) the proportion of significantly correlated genetic loci with positive correlation estimated using LAVA91 (Supplementary Fig. 9-3).

Conditional analysis and fine-mapping. We identified potential independent genome-wide significant lead variants for each of the broadly defined genome-wide-significant loci of our GWAS meta-analysis results104 using the following approach. For each genome-wide significant SNP we defined the associated LD region by recording the left and rightmost variant with $r ^ { 2 } { \geq } 0 . 1$ , assigning the SNP with the lowest P value within each LD clump as the index SNP. Index SNPs were considered independent when $r ^ { 2 } < 0 . 1$ within 3-Mb windows. To define GWAS loci, a 50-kb window was added on each side of the LD region and overlapping LD regions were combined into a single locus, assigning the index SNP with the lowest P value as lead SNP. Only a single SNP was kept from within the major histocompatibility complex region, due to extended LD and a strong association signal of the major histocompatibility complex region (chr 6:25–35 Mb).

To identify additional independent index variants, we performed a stepwise model selection procedure to select independently associated SNPs implemented in GCTA-COJO29,30.

We assigned posterior probabilities (PPs) of being causal to SNPs and constructed credible sets of SNPs that cumulatively capture $9 5 \%$ of the regional $\mathsf { P P } ^ { 1 2 5 }$ using PAINTOR126–128, CAVIARBF and FINEMAP129, using the CAUSALdb fine-mapping pipeline (https://github.com/mulinlab/ CAUSALdb-finemapping-pip). We applied a conservative approach and assumed one causal variant for each locus.

Colocalization analyses were performed to evaluate the extent of overlap between eQTL and GWAS signatures in DEP and to identify putative causal genes from GWAS associations. Considering only the $9 5 \%$ credible SNPs from the fine-mapped DEP meta-analysis, we integrated GWAS results and eQTL data from a previous meta-analysis integrating signals among three brain datasets53 applying a fixed-effects model. The eQTL data originate from an eQTL meta-analysis on RNA-sequenced gene expression data from the DLPFC from PsychENCODE50 and ROSMAP130, and from 13 brain regions from GTEx131.

Using the Coloc method54,55, we extracted eQTL signals of genes within 200 kb distance to significant GWAS variants $( P < 5 \times 1 0 ^ { - 8 } )$ ) using effect sizes $\boldsymbol { \beta }$ -values) and s.e. from eQTL and GWAS as input. Four partially hierarchical hypotheses55 were tested: H0, no association; H1, GWAS association only; H2, eQTL association only; H3, both GWAS and eQTL association but no colocalization; and H4, both GWAS and eQTL association and colocalization, considering posterior probability for H4 (PPH4) ${ \tt > } 0 . 8$ as strong evidence from both GWAS, eQTL and colocalization. In addition, we conducted the eCAVIAR approach56, in which both eQTL and GWAS were fine-mapped and the product of PP (CLPP) was calculated, prioritizing genes with at least a single variant with CLPP $\geq 0 . 0 1$ .

Gene-wise and pathway analysis. We used a number of different approaches and data, including those available via the FUMA $\mathsf { v } . 1 . 3 . 6 \mathsf { a } ^ { 4 8 }$ website (http://fuma.ctglab.nl) for downstream annotation and functional characterization of significant loci.

We performed a genome-wide gene-based association analysis using the MAGMA tool, as implemented in FUMA ${ \mathsf { v } } . 1 . 3 . 6 { \mathsf { a } } ^ { 4 8 }$ . Using this we mapped SNPs from the DEP GWAS meta-analysis to 17,840 protein-coding genes, and performed Bonferroni correction for the total number of protein-coding genes $( P < 2 . 8 \times 1 0 ^ { - 6 }$ ; Supplementary Table 7d).

Protein-coding genes were mapped if they were located within $1 0 \boldsymbol { \mathrm { k b } }$ up- or downstream from index variants or if a credible variant was annotated to the gene based on eQTL data or chromatin interaction data from human brain (datasets used in the mapping are listed in Supplementary Table 7a). No additional variant filtering by functional annotation was applied in the eQTL and chromatin interaction mapping. This analysis identified 411 DEP risk genes, which were used in a gene-set enrichment analysis within the GENE2FUNC module of FUMA, where we analyzed 8,664 gene sets derived from GO Biological Process $\scriptstyle ( n = 7 , 6 5 8$ ; 393 gene sets) and GO Cellular Components $_ { ( n = 1 , 0 0 6 }$ ; 86 gene sets) ontology in MSigDB.

Tissue and cell-type enrichment. We also used FUMA to perform tissue expression analyses on data available through their website, by testing whether the identified genetic associations for both the primary and narrow DEP phenotype definitions were enriched regarding transcriptome profiles of human tissues using summary statistics based on all SNPs. Finally, we used FUMA to perform cell-type enrichment analyses48 based on the DEP GWAS summary statistics. We use MAGMA gene-property analysis to test cell-type specificity of the DEP phenotype using GWAS summary statistics. For MAGMA gene-property analysis with single-cell RNA-seq, the gene-property analysis aims to test for relationships between cell-specific gene expression profiles and disease–gene associations. In all the above-mentioned analyses implemented in FUMA, default settings were applied.

To further evaluate whether the genomic loci implicated in DEP are enriched in any particular cell type, we intersected common DEP risk variants with two recent epigenomic maps of cell-specific open chromatin57,5 8 using an LD score partitioned heritability approach59 (Fig. 3b).

Transcriptome-wide association study. In addition, we performed a TWAS, imputing the genetically regulated gene expression using EpiXcan49 and using models trained on the PsychENCODE Consortium50,51 expression data for genes and transcripts detected in the DLPFC, with the aim of identifying and prioritizing putative causal loci for the broad DEP phenotype definition. A total of 34,646 genes/transcripts were tested (transcripts with prediction performance $R ^ { 2 } > 0 . 0 1$ and prediction performance q value ${ < } 0 . 0 5$ with the Benjamini–Hochberg method were retained) and applying a significance threshold of $P { < } 1 . 4 4 \times 1 0 ^ { - 6 }$ (corresponding to Bonferroni correction of all genes and isoforms tested; Fig. 1d, Extended Data Fig. 1, Supplementary Fig. 10 and Supplementary Table 9), using information on approximately independent LD blocks in human populations52 to identify independent genomic regions with genes/transcripts showing significant differential gene expression between DEP cases and controls.

Polygenic risk scores. Using available summary statistics form published GWAS as training datasets, we calculated PRSs for individuals in the iPSYCH2015 sample using LDpred2 (ref. 132). Summary statistics were filtered for an imputation info score $\mathbf { I N F O } \geq 0 . 9$ if available. When using external summary stats not processed using Ricopili or imputed using different imputation references, we excluded all ambiguous markers to avoid potential strand conflicts. To improve performance of the scores and avoid including artifacts from batch effects, we restricted the summary statistics to include only SNPs known to be present in both the iPSYCH2012 and iPSYCH2015i data at a reasonable quality (info score 0.6 and MAF 0.05). This step also checked for allele flips.

To derive a PRS for DEP within the iPSYCH2015 sample we used the DEP meta-analysis12,14,23,24 excluding all iPSYCH samples as training. We also calculated PRS based on GWAS summary statistics from $\mathsf { B P } ^ { \mathrm { 1 1 8 } }$ , $\ S Z ^ { 1 2 1 }$ , ADHD13 and $\mathsf { A S D } ^ { 1 5 }$ . The PRS for ADHD was based on a combination of ‘external’ GWAS sumstats (without iPSYCH data) and ‘internal’ training in the iPSYCH sample. The iPSYCH sample was split into ten groups and in ten iterations of leave-one-out the PRS was calculated for individuals in each of the ten groups using a GWAS performed on the remaining nine groups as training. A fixed-effects variance-weighted meta-analysis, implemented in METAL28, of two GWAS for ANX, one based on self-report of physician diagnosis of ANX in the ${ \mathsf { M V P } } ^ { 1 1 2 }$ and the other being core ANX in the FinnGen cohort22 (see above), served as weights for generating an ANX PRS summarizing genetic risk of ANX. To derive a PRS for neuroticism we used GWAS summary statistics of a weighted neuroticism sum-score, constructed by adding up the ten individual item responses (fed-up, guilt, irr, miss, mood, tense, nerv feel, suf nerv, worry emb and worry) by Nagel et al.120. In addition, we calculated PRSs using the following GWAS summary statistics for traits related to SU and SUD as training datasets. These include cannabis use disorder39(excluding iPSYCH samples; training only based on data from PGC and data from decode genetics), cannabis use114, alcohol dependence115, drinks per week, smoking ever116, age of initiation as a regular smoker, current versus a former smoker (smoking cessation), number of cigarettes per day and smoking initiation117. All summary statistics were combined in a pseudo-meta-analysis, using the fixed-effects variance-weighted meta-analysis implemented in METAL28, to generate a SUD PRS summarizing genetic liability of SUD.

With the aim at improving the PRS prediction we attempted to exploit the genetic overlap of DEP with other phenotypes. We therefore combined all the above PRSs into a single score as a weighted sum15. We chose here to use the log(OR) for the logistic regression of subphenotype of interest on each score as a continuous factor as a measure of ‘importance’ in context of the subphenotype of interest (either recurrent versus single-episode or DEP with additional diagnosis of BP, SZ or SUD). The logistic regressions were adjusted for the first ten PCs of the second PCA mentioned above. We added each standardized PRS weighted by its log(OR) one PRS at a time to the aggregated score. We started with the phenotype with highest abs $( \log ( \mathrm { O R } ) )$ and ended with the lowest. This way we ended up with a sequence of scores starting with $S _ { 0 }$ and continuing with:

$$
S _ { k } = \frac { \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { k } \mathrm { l o g } \left( \mathrm { O R } _ { P _ { \mathrm { i } } } \right) \mathsf { S } _ { P _ { \mathrm { i } } } - \mu \left( \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { k } \mathrm { l o g } \left( \mathrm { O R } _ { P _ { \mathrm { i } } } \right) \mathsf { S } _ { P _ { \mathrm { i } } } \right) } { \sigma \left( \sum _ { i = 1 } ^ { k } \mathrm { l o g } \left( \mathrm { O R } _ { P _ { \mathrm { i } } } \right) \mathsf { S } _ { P _ { \mathrm { i } } } \right) }
$$

where $S _ { P _ { i } }$ is the score for phenotype $P _ { \mathrm { i } }$ , $\mathrm { O R } _ { P _ { \mathrm { i } } }$ the OR from the logistic regression of subphenotype of interest on $S _ { P \mathrm { \uparrow } }$ as a continuous factor while adjusting for ten PCs, $\mu$ is the mean and $\sigma$ is the s.d.

To examine potential polygenic heterogeneity across DEP sub-phenotypes, we investigated how PRS trained on the different phenotypes described above were distributed across DEP sub-phenotypes in iPSYCH using a multivariate PRS approach both in a sex-stratified and in an unstratified analysis. The method is described in detail by Grove et al.15, and is a regression of multiple outcome variables, and in principle a linear regression for each PRS on the DEP sub-phenotypes allowing for comparisons on the average PRS across sub-phenotypes for PRS from a number of phenotypes adjusting for necessary covariates. As the variance–covariance matrix is fitted jointly and accounting for the covariance, the inherent correlation between scores is adjusted when estimating variance and in the hypothesis testing. We performed four mvPRS analyses testing for differences among DEP subtypes; individuals with DEP who developed (1) ANX, (2) BP, (3) SZ or (4) SUD. Each of the four multiple regression analyses tests for individual and distinct hypotheses, one for each PRS and the reported P values were not corrected for multiple testing. BP cases were excluded from the analyses not involving BP.

In addition, we applied a Cox proportional hazard model to estimate HRRs and absolute risk of developing (1) a second episode of DEP, (2) ANX, (3) BP, (4) SZ and (5) SUD, among individuals already being diagnosed with their first episode of DEP. Analyses were stratified by PRS deciles of the phenotypes described above and using functions from the R packages survival (https://CRAN.R-project.org/package=su rvival) 133,134, while correcting for batch (iPSYCH2012 and iPSYCH2015i) and PCs. The Cox proportional hazard models were stratified into three groups based on first, second to ninth, and tenth deciles.

The middle second-to-ninth decile group (the $80 \%$ prediction interval of the PRS) was used as a reference. Two-sided $9 5 \%$ confidence intervals (CIs) were obtained for the stratified absolute risk trajectories using Cox proportional hazards model with covariates135, taking the mean of each covariate (PC1–5 and iPSYCH sample) and using the survfit.coxph function implemented in the survival R package.

All Cox analyses were conducted for both sexes combined and for females and males separately. In addition, HRRs and absolute risk of developing (1) ANX, (2) BP, (3) SZ and (4) SUD were estimated in the iPSYCH subcohort, excluding all DEP cases, to obtain estimates in the general Danish population for comparison with the iPSYCH DEP cohort.

DEP PRS in the Philadelphia Neurodevelopmental Cohort. DEP PRSs were calculated for 4,973 individuals of European ancestry from the PNC65,66 using the primary DEP GWAS meta-analysis (and narrowly defined DEP GWAS meta-analysis) of the present study as training. Genotypes from the first PNC release (dbGaP phs000607.v1.p1) and neurocognitive phenotypes from the third release (dbGaP, phs000607. v3.p2) were utilized. Individuals whose genotypically inferred and phenotypically reported sex did not match, those who did not meet the an identity-by-descent filter $\scriptstyle { \hat { \pi } } > 0 . 1 8 5 )$ ), those who did not meet the individual-level missingness filter of 0.05 and those with heterozygosity rates ${ \pm } 3 \times { \mathsf { s . d . } }$ . from the mean were removed. SNPs that failed to meet the Hardy–Weinberg proportions, MAF and SNP-level missingness filters of 0.00001, 0.01 and 0.05, respectively, were also removed. Genotype imputation was performed using the reference panel HRC r1.1 2016 on the Michigan Imputation Server (https://imputationserver. sph.umich.edu/index.html#!), selecting the ‘Mixed population’ option. Imputed SNPs with an imputation $R ^ { 2 } < 0 . 3$ and those who failed to meet the missingness, MAF and Hardy–Weinberg proportion thresholds stated above were removed. The first two multi-dimensional scaling dimensions were plotted to identify and remove outlier genotypes. The GemTools package in $\mathsf { R } ^ { 1 3 6 , 1 3 7 }$ and Ward’s hierarchical clustering methods were used to identify individuals of European ancestry.

PRS ${ \mathsf { C S } } ^ { 1 3 8 }$ was used to apply continuous shrinkage priors to the effect sizes from DEP GWAS summary statistics. A European LD reference panel provided by the developers of PRS CS was utilized (https:// github.com/getian107/PRScs), which draws from the 1000 Genomes Project data. The following PRS CS default settings were used: parameter a in the γ-γ prior ${ \bf \mu } = { \bf 1 } { \bf \Lambda }$ , parameter $^ b$ in the γ-γ prior $= 0 . 5$ , MCMC iterations $\mathbf { \tau } = 1 , 0 0 0$ , number of burn-in iterations $= 5 0 0$ and thinning of the Markov chain factor $= 5 .$ . The global shrinkage parameter phi was set using a fully Bayesian determination method. Individual-level DEP PRSs were calculated using PLINK v.2.0 (ref. 139). The associations between scaled (mean 0, s.d. 1) DEP PRS and 15 scaled (mean 0, s.d. 1) neurocognitive phenotypes in the PNC were assessed using linear regression of phenotypes on DEP PRS.

The neurocognitive phenotypes included performance on the $\mathsf { C N B } ^ { 6 7 }$ , as well as results from WRAT-4.

A $\sqrt { k - x }$ transformation (where k is a constant) was performed on CNB measures of age differentiation, emotion differentiation, spatial memory and verbal reasoning, as their distributions were left-skewed. A $\log _ { 1 0 } ( k - x )$ -transformation was performed on CNB measures of abstraction and mental flexibility, attention, verbal memory and working memory, as their distributions were left-skewed. A $\mathsf { l o g } _ { 1 0 } ( x )$ transformation was performed on the WRAT-4 measure, whereas a $1 / x$ transformation was performed on the CNB measure of sensorimotor processing, as their distributions were right-skewed. For the CNB measure of emotion identification, a $\sqrt { k - x }$ transformation was performed on the PEIT-AB form of the task and a $\log _ { 1 0 } ( k - x )$ transformation was performed on the PEIT-C, PEIT-D and PEIT-E forms of the task, each owing to their left-skew. For the CNB measure of nonverbal reasoning, a $\overbrace { { \sqrt { k - x } } }$ transformation was performed on the PMAT-18B form of the task owing to its left-skew, whereas a $\sqrt { x }$ transformation was performed on the PMAT-24A form of the task owing to its right-skew. No transformations were performed on CNB measures of facial memory, finger-tapping speed and spatial reasoning. Neurocognitive phenotype scores were multiplied by $^ { - 1 }$ , following data transformation and scaling, when needed so that higher scores always indicate better performance across the assessed tasks.

Covariates included in the analysis were age at neurocognitive testing, age squared, the first ten multi-dimensional scaling dimensions, sex and genotyping batch. $R ^ { 2 }$ was used to report the total variance explained by DEP PRS and model covariates for the 15 tested neurocognitive phenotypes. Additionally, a variance partitioning as implemented in the calcVarpart() function of the variancePartition software140 (https://bioconductor.org/packages/variancePartition) was used to determine the variance explained by DEP PRS and each covariate individually. FDR-adjusted $P$ values were reported.

Ancestry outliers. Ancestry outliers $( - 1 0 \% )$ were removed to avoid confounding from population substructure and admixture. To assess the generalizability of our main results, we conducted DEP GWAS of the ancestry outliers using Ricopili and adjusting for the first ten PCs (Supplementary Fig. 24a,b). This GWAS showed a high genetic correlation $( r _ { \mathrm { G } } = 0 . 9 5$ , s.e. 0.24, $P { = } 1 \times 1 0 ^ { - 4 }$ ) with our primary DEP meta-analysis as shown in Supplementary Table 23. Sign-tests of overall replication of the direction of effects for various GWAS $P$ value thresholds using our primary meta-analysis as a discovery sample and the iPSYCH2015 ancestry outliers as replication sample, showed an overall replication ratio of $0 . 5 9 \substack { - 0 . 6 4 }$ (Supplementary Table 24). This suggests that many more findings would likely replicate, provided a much larger and more powerful sample, in other non-European ancestries. Previous GWAS of DEP using transancestral meta-analysis24 generally supports the transferability of GWAS results across ancestries.

In addition, we performed the multivariate PRS and the Cox proportional hazard analyses of recurrent DEP using the ancestry outlier sample (Supplementary Figs. 25–28 and Supplementary Tables 25–28d), adjusting all analyses for the first ten PCs and iPSYCH2012/ iPSYCH2015i. The ancestry outlier mvPRS analyses (Supplementary Fig. 25) showed similar directions of differences in DEP PRS and neuroticism PRS for single versus recurrent DEP as in the corresponding analysis of the sample with European ancestry (Supplementary Fig. 16-1 and Supplementary Table 16a), although none of the differences appeared to be significant (Supplementary Table 25). For ANX PRS and BP PRS, however, the observed differences in the European sample were not reproduced for the ancestry outliers. Likewise, for the Cox regression analyses, the observed differences in HRR and absolute risk of developing a second episode of DEP between the tenth and middle DEP PRS and DEP SUM PRS deciles in the European ancestry sample (Supplementary Fig. 17-1) were not reproduced in the ancestry outliers (Supplementary Fig. 26).

Regarding psychiatric comorbidity among individuals with DEP, a similar pattern of increased load of ANX PRS, SZ PRS and neuroticism PRS among individuals with DEP with comorbidity for ANX was observed in the European ancestry (Extended Data Fig. 2 and Supplementary Table 18a) sample and in the ancestry outlier sample, although this was not significant in the latter except for neuroticism PRS (Supplementary Fig. 27a and Supplementary Table 27a). For individuals with DEP with/without BP, the same direction of differences observed significantly in the European sample (Extended Data Fig. 3 and Supplementary Table 18d) was observed for BP PRS and SZ PRS in the sample of ancestry outliers, but only significantly so for the BP PRS $\scriptstyle P = 0 . 0 1 3$ ; Supplementary Fig. 27b and Supplementary Table 27b). The direction of differences was not reproduced for the DEP PRS and ASD PRS. Regarding comorbidity with SZ, the same direction of differences in DEP PRS, SZ PRS, ADHD PRS, ASD PRS, neuroticism PRS, SU PRS and SUD PRS, among individuals with DEP with/without SZ, as significantly observed in the European sample (Extended Data Fig. 4 and Supplementary Table $1 8 \mathsf { g }$ ), was also observed in the sample of ancestry outliers, whereas the difference seemed to be the opposite for the BP PRS (Supplementary Fig. 27c and Supplementary Table 27c). The direction of differences significantly observed in the European sample, with increased PRS among individuals with DEP with SUD compared to those without (Extended Data Fig. 5 and Supplementary Table 18J), was reproduced in the ancestry outlier sample, except for the neuroticism PRS (Supplementary Fig. 27d and Supplementary Table 27d).

The general pattern of differences in HRR and absolute risk of developing comorbidity for SZ and SUD in the tenth compared to the middle deciles, that was observed in the European sample (Extended Data Figs. 8 and 9 and Supplementary Tables 21a and 22a) was also reflected in SZ SUM PRS and SUD SUM PRS of the ancestry outliers (Supplementary Figs. 28-3 and 28-4 and Supplementary Table 28c,d). Notably, there was a tendency for a higher risk of developing SUD among individuals with DEP in the tenth decile compared to the middle deciles of the BP PRS, SU PRS and SUD PRS, although this was less clear for the DEP PRS, ANX PRS and SZ PRS (Supplementary Fig. 28-4 and Supplementary Table 28d). Regarding risk of comorbidity for ANX and transitioning into BP, the differences between the tenth and middle deciles observed in the European sample (Extended Data Figs. 6 and 7 and Supplementary Table 19a and 20a) was, however, not clearly reflected in the ancestry outliers (Supplementary Figs. 28-1 and 28-2 and Supplementary Table 28a,b).

Reporting on sex and gender

1) The research findings reported apply to both sexes, and sex is not mentioned in the title. There are, however, notable sexdifferences in some of absolute risk estimates, we have mentioned this in the revised abstract.   
2) Within the iPSYCH sample, sex was assigned based on information from the national registers and subsequently checked using genetic markers on the X chromosome. Sex-stratified version of all the analyses is not possible with the available external datasets (summary statistics for each sex are rarely available). This is specified in the method section of the revised manuscript.   
3) Analyses are reported disaggregated for sex where this information is available, i.e. within iPSYCH data. Disaggregated numbers are reported for each analysis. iPSYCH data contains a total of 22996 female cases and 22108 female controls, while there are 11099 male case and 23285 male controls.   
We have conducted sex-stratified versions of all the iPSYCH specific analyses, i.e. the multivariate-PRS and Cox proportional hazard analyses (Supplementary Tables S17-S22 and Supplementary Figures S18, S20-S25). These show only minor genetic differences between males and females, with some large sex differences in the absolute risk for some sub-phenotypes and PRS, but with the same relative difference between decile groups for males and females. For example, the risk of substance use disorder (SUD) among depression cases the Hazard Rate Ratios are very similar, but the absolute risk trajectories are higher for males. The relative difference between the two extreme deciles and the middle deciles is more or less the same for the two sexes, thus suggesting that the genetic effects are the same. This sex-specific difference is thus likely due to other factors than the autosomal risk factors considered in the PRS used in the current analyses.   
That PRS loadings are similar for males and females across psychiatric disorders, has also previously been shown in crossdisorder analyses [Martin, J. et al. Examining Sex-Differentiated Genetic Effects Across Neuropsychiatric and Behavioral Traits. Biol Psychiatry 89, 1127-1137 (2021). https://doi.org:10.1016/j.biopsych.2020.12.024], which conclude that sex differences in the common autosomal genetic architecture of neuropsychiatric and behavioral phenotypes are small and polygenic and unlikely to fully account for observed sex-differentiated attributes, which is in accordance with our sexstratified analyses.

In the meta-analysis we included the internal iPSYCH and FinnGen sample, in addition to public available GWAS summary statistics. The iPSYCH2015 sample is a nation-wide population sample extracted from a baseline cohort consisting of all children born in Denmark between May 1, 1981, and December 31, 2008, who were alive and resided in Denmark on their one-year birthday. The iPSYCH cohort includes relatively young individuals (mean age of depression cases 26.97 years, $S \mathsf { D } { = } 5 . 2 6$ , and controls 22.2 years, $S _ { \mathrm { { D } = 7 . 2 4 } }$ , at follow-up December 31st 2016) with $67 \%$ being females among cases and $49 \%$ among random controls. All analyzes were adjusted for batch, and principal components included to control for population stratification. The FinnGen cohort includes controls with a mean age of 54.6 years $S _ { \mathrm { { D } = 1 6 } . 8 } ^ { \prime }$ ) with $56 \%$ females and $4 4 \%$ males.

# Sample size

No sample size calculation was made. Previous studies of psychiatric disorders that are very polygenic (e.g. major depression, schizophrenia) have demonstrated that high numbers of cases and controls (in line with the sample size analyzed in this study) yield enough power to detect common risk variants with low effect sizes. The iPSYCH2015 sample is a nation-wide population sample extracted from a baseline cohort consisting of all children born in Denmark between May 1, 1981, and December 31, 2008, who were alive and resided in Denmark on their one-year birthday, and who have a known mother[1,2]. Individuals diagnosed with one (or more) of six major psychiatric disorders (ADHD, autism, depression, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, anorexia) were identified via the Danish Psychiatric Central Research Register[3], which includes data on all individuals treated in Denmark at psychiatric hospitals (from 1969 onward) as well as at outpatient psychiatric clinics (from 1995 onward). A random sample from the same birth cohort were chosen as control group. The iPSYCH2015 cohort consists of the initial case-cohort sample iPSYCH201225 and the recent extension iPSYCH2015i[1]. Individuals with a ICD10 F32-F33 diagnosis in 2016 or earlier were considered as depression cases and the random population-based sample excluding individuals with a depression diagnosis were used as controls.

# Data exclusions

Individuals with BP may initially be diagnosed with depression, and to avoid confounding of the depression phenotype used, all individuals with a diagnosis of BP were excluded for all analyses not involving BP, i.e. analyses involving co-occurrence of MD and BP diagnosis. When defining later depression episodes only contacts with the ICD-10 codes: F32, F33.0-F33.3, F33.8-F33.9 were considered. Individuals with the ICD-10 code F33.4 “Major depressive disorder, recurrent, in remission” were omitted because these contacts are unlikely to indicate a new episode of depression. Only ICD-10: F32, F33.0-F33.3, F33.8-F33.9 diagnosis at dates later than 60 days after the end date for all previous ICD10: F32-F33 diagnosis were considered as new episodes of depression, thus categorizing that particular individual as having recurrent depression.

# Replication

All available data were combined into a large meta-analysis, and replication of results have therefore not been possible. Consistency was checked internally between iPSYCH2012, iPSYCH2015i and external GWAS summary stats. We found good consistency across datasets and have no reason to believe that our results were significantly influenced by technical artifacts or other errors.

# Randomization

It is an observational study comparing, comparing cases and controls, based on diagnoses obtained from registers and a random populationbased sample from the complement as controls, and there was no randomization as such.

Blinding

In iPSYCH, diagnoses are drawn from registries. These are administrative data bases populated by data from the clinicians long before the current study. The blood samples are pulled from a biobank. Hence, the study participants and diagnosing clinicians are blinded with respect to this study. Genotyping is done on a massive scale on 134.000 individuals on 500.000 variables for the overall iPSYCH experiment (which by imputation is expanded to ${ \sim } 1 0$ million variables), and the data is generated without a specif goal or effect in mind except for an overall goal of investigating the genetic and environmental effects on psychiatric disorders. So although it is in principle possible for analysts in the lab to look up crude diagnostic data for a sample, it will not change the genotyping. - In the meta analysis we include data from the Psychiatric Genetics Consortium (PGC) and other collaborators which has been reported in an earlier publication. Their design was different, but analyses analogous.
