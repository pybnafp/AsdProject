# BACKGROUND

Antidepressants are used to treat acute depression in patients with bipolar I disorder, but their effect as maintenance treatment after the remission of depression has not been well studied.

# METHODS

We conducted a multisite, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial of maintenance of treatment with adjunctive escitalopram or bupropion XL as compared with discontinuation of antidepressant therapy in patients with bipolar I disorder who had recently had remission of a depressive episode. Patients were randomly assigned in a 1:1 ratio to continue treatment with antidepressants for 52 weeks after remission or to switch to placebo at 8 weeks. The primary outcome, assessed in a time-to-event analysis, was any mood episode, as defined by scores on scales measuring symptoms of hypomania or mania, depression, suicidality, and mood-episode severity; additional treatment or hospitalization for mood symptoms; or attempted or completed suicide. Key secondary outcomes included the time to an episode of mania or hypomania or depression.

# RESULTS

Of 209 patients with bipolar I disorder who participated in an open-label treatment phase, 150 who had remission of depression were enrolled in the double-blind phase in addition to 27 patients who were enrolled directly. A total of 90 patients were assigned to continue treatment with the prescribed antidepressant for 52 weeks (52-week group) and 87 were assigned to switch to placebo at 8 weeks (8-week group). The trial was stopped before full recruitment was reached owing to slow recruitment and funding limitations. At 52 weeks, 28 of the patients in the 52-week group $( 3 1 \% )$ and 40 in the 8-week group $( 4 6 \% )$ had a primary-outcome event. The hazard ratio for time to any mood episode in the 52-week group relative to the 8-week group was 0.68 $( 9 5 \%$ confidence interval [CI], 0.43 to 1.10; $\mathrm { P = } 0 . 1 2$ by log-rank test). A total of 11 patients in the 52-week group $( 1 2 \% )$ as compared with 5 patients in the 8-week group $( 6 \% )$ had mania or hypomania (hazard ratio, 2.28; $9 5 \%$ CI, 0.86 to 6.08), and 15 patients $( 1 7 \% )$ as compared with 35 patients $( 4 0 \% )$ had recurrence of depression (hazard ratio, 0.43; $9 5 \%$ CI, 0.25 to 0.75). The incidence of adverse events was similar in the two groups.

# CONCLUSIONS

In a trial involving patients with bipolar I disorder and a recently remitted depressive episode, adjunctive treatment with escitalopram or bupropion XL that continued for 52 weeks did not show a significant benefit as compared with treatment for 8 weeks in preventing relapse of any mood episode. The trial was stopped early owing to slow recruitment and funding limitations. (Funded by the Canadian Institutes of Health Research; ClinicalTrials.gov number, NCT00958633.)

LTHOUGH MANIA IS THE DEFINING FEAture of bipolar I disorder (as compared with bipolar II disorder), patients with this disorder have depressive symptoms three times more frequently than manic symptoms.1-3 The effect of depressive symptoms on health and functioning is at least as severe as that of manic episodes.4 Furthermore, some studies have shown that suicide attempts and suicide deaths are at least 18 times as common during depressive episodes as during manic episodes in bipolar I disorder.5,6 Olanzapine—fluoxetine, quetiapine, lurasidone, cariprazine, and lumateperone are medications approved by the Food and Drug Administration for the treatment of acute depressive episodes in bipolar I disorder.

Apart from lamotrigine,8 quetiapine, and lithium, other FDA-approved maintenance treatments for bipolar I disorder have limited efficacy in preventing depression,10,11 and depressive relapses are common among persons with this condition. Adjunctive antidepressants are commonly used to treat depression in patients with bipolar I disorder; as many as $5 7 \%$ of patients with bipolar I disorder received prescriptions for adjunctive antidepressants from 2013 through 2016,12,13and some studies have shown that $8 0 \%$ of patients continue receiving antidepressants for 6 months or longer after remission.14 There are limited data from randomized trials assessing the efficacy and safety of antidepressants when administered as adjuncts to mood-stabilizing medications or second-generation antipsychotic agents in the maintenance treatment of bipolar I disorder.

We conducted a trial involving patients with bipolar I disorder who had recently had remission from a depressive episode in which we assessed the strategy of continuing adjunctive treatment with escitalopram or bupropion XL in addition to a mood stabilizer or second-generation antipsychotic agent (or a combination of the two) for 52 weeks as compared with tapering and discontinuing the use of antidepressants at 8 weeks.

# TRIAL PROCEDURES AND INTERVENTIONS

Patients who were in the midst of an acute bipolar I depressive episode when they entered the initial open-label phase received treatment with adjunctive escitalopram or bupropion XL and were assessed every two weeks for up to 16 weeks to determine their eligibility for the double-blind phase, including remission from their depressive episode. Patients who met the eligibility criteria were randomly assigned in a 1:1 ratio to continue treatment with their prescribed antidepressants for 8 weeks or for 52 weeks; randomization was performed in permuted blocks of four, with stratification according to type of antidepressant (escitalopram or bupropion XL), antimanic agents, and country. Patients assigned to the 8-week group continued to receive their prescribed antidepressant for the first 6 weeks after randomization, with tapering of the drug dose beginning at the end of week 6 and leading to full substitution with placebo from week 8 onward for the remaining 44 weeks.

Placebo tablets were designed to be identical in appearance to the trial medication that the patient had been receiving at week 6. Patients assigned to the 52-week group continued to receive their prescribed antidepressant for 52 weeks. The antidepressant dose could be reduced if there were unacceptable side effects (as determined by the site investigator) but could not be increased.

During the double-blind phase, all patients continued to receive the same mood-stabilizing or second-generation antipsychotic medication (or both) that they had been receiving at the time of enrollment. Dose adjustments were permitted only if unacceptable side effects developed or if the serum levels of the medications fell outside the therapeutic range (i.e., 0.6 to $1 . 2 \ \mathrm { m m o l }$ per liter for lithium, 50 to $1 0 0 ~ \mu \mathrm { g }$ per milliliter for valproic acid, or 5 to $1 2 ~ \mathrm { m g }$ per liter for carbamazepine). Patients could not receive any additional psychotropic medication except lorazepam, clonazepam, temazepam, or zopiclone to treat insomnia. The treating clinicians, site investigators, research personnel, and patients were unaware of the group assignments. Patients were assessed by trained raters (who were unaware of the group assignments) every 2 weeks from baseline to week

12, and then every 4 weeks to week 52 or when a primary-outcome event occurred was reached (as measured with the use of the YMRS, MADRS, CGI-S-BD, and CGI-Improvement-BD tools).

Adherence to the medication or placebo regimen was assessed with the use of patient interviews and pill counts. Vital signs, weight, and hip and waist measurements were recorded at each visit, and blood samples were obtained for measurement of serum levels of lithium or valproate at baseline, week 24, final visit, and as clinically indicated. The occurrence and resolution of adverse events were documented with the use of open-ended questions asked by trial clinicians and the UBC Mood Disorders Centre Side-Effects Rating Scale questionnaires developed by the investigators and completed by patients at each visit.

# OUTCOMES

The primary outcome, assessed in a time-to-event analysis, was any mood episode, defined as any of the following: a YMRS score of at least 16 (mild mania), a MADRS score of at least 20 (moderate depression), a CGI-S-BD score of at least 4 for mania or depression (moderately ill), hospitalization for mood symptoms, necessity of additional pharmacotherapy for emerging mood symptoms, a MADRS suicide item score of at least 4 (scores range from 0 to 6, with higher scores indicating greater suicide risk), or a suicide attempt or suicide death. Patients who had a primary-outcome event were excluded from further follow-up and received subsequent appropriate care. Primaryoutcome events were classified as manic or hypomanic, depressive, or mixed on the basis of the CGI-BD, YMRS, and MADRS scores as well as assessment by the site investigator. Secondary outcomes were time to a depressive episode, a manic or hypomanic episode, discontinuation from the trial for any clinical reason (e.g., occurrence of a mood event, withdrawal of informed consent, or adverse event), any mood episode or subsyndromal symptoms, time spent in these episodes, and scores on the aforementioned clinical rating scales. Details regarding the prespecified outcomes are provided in the protocol.

# STATISTICAL ANALYSIS

Using the method of Lachin and Foulkes,18 we estimated that 216 patients (108 per group) would provide the trial with $8 0 \%$ power at a twosided alpha level of 0.05; the estimate was based on an absolute between-group difference of 20 percentage points $5 0 \%$ in the 52-week group vs. $3 0 \%$ in the 8-week group), allowing for a cumulative $2 5 \%$ loss to withdrawal from the trial. Owing to slower-than-anticipated recruitment and expiration of funding, recruitment stopped after 178 patients were enrolled.

Analyses were conducted on a modified intention-to-treat basis and included all patients who underwent randomization except one patient who had missing data at baseline and follow-up. We used Kaplan—Meier survival curves to summarize the time to any mood episode. The statistical analysis plan (available with the protocol) prespecified the use of a Cox proportional-hazards model, with adjustments for age, sex, and drug combination, to compare the primary outcome between the groups. However, the data violated the proportional-hazards assumption, and the analysis was changed to a log-rank test with a corresponding $9 5 \%$ confidence interval. Data from patients who did not have a primary-outcome event were censored as of the time of the last follow-up visit. Multiple imputation of censored outcomes was used post hoc to assess the potential effect of these missing values. Because the patients in both groups continued to receive treatment with antidepressants during the first 6 weeks of the trial, treatment effects could presumably arise only after 6 weeks, when treatment differed between the two groups. Therefore, we conducted a prespecified sensitivity analysis of the primary outcome that included only events that occurred after the first 6 weeks rather than all the events that occurred over the 52-week duration of the trial.19

Log-rank tests and $9 5 \%$ confidence intervals were also used to evaluate the secondary outcomes of a depressive episode and a manic episode, assessed in a time-to-event analysis. In the analysis of these outcomes, data from patients who had either episode were censored at the time of the episode. Because there was no prespecified plan for adjustment of the widths of confidence intervals for multiple comparisons for secondary outcomes, no definite conclusions can be drawn from these results.

# ANTIDEPRESSANT SUBGROUP ANALYSIS

Our trial was not powered for subgroup analyses and results are descriptive only (Table S6). In the escitalopram subgroup, depressive events occurred in 9 patients in the 52-week group and 19 in the 8-week group; manic events were observed in 9 patients and 3 patients, respectively. In the bupropion XL subgroup, 6 patients in the 52-week and 16 patients in the 8-week group had depressive episodes. Manic episodes occurred in 2 patients in each group; 2 patients in the 52-week group, as compared with none in the 8-week group, had mixed episodes. There were no suicide attempts or suicide deaths in either group, but one patient in the 8-week group had worsening of suicidal ideation and met the criterion for a component of the primary outcome.

# SAFETY

The incidence of adverse events was similar in the two groups, with a low rate of discontinuation due to adverse events (Table 3). No serious adverse events were observed. Clinically significant weight gain $( { \geq } 7 \%$ increase in body weight) was observed in $1 4 \%$ of patients in the 52-week group and $7 \%$ of patients in the 8-week group.

There is evidence from previous studies that a patient's mood state before entry into a study of maintenance therapy predicts the type of relapse of mood episode the patient may have.2° In a finding consistent with this evidence, three times as many patients in the trial had depressive relapses (50 patients; 15 in the 52-week group and 35 in the 8-week group) as had manic relapses (16 patients; 11 in the 52-week group and 5 in the 8-week group) during the double-blind phase; this difference was predictable because patients were recruited after remission from a recent depressive episode.

Although antidepressants are widely used in the treatment of bipolar disorder,12-14 their use has been controversial owing to limited data supporting short-term or long-term efficacy and concerns about the possibility that these drugs may induce mania, mixed states, or rapid cycling between mania and depression.21-25 A meta-analysis of six randomized trials showed that adjunctive modern antidepressant therapy was effective for acute depression in bipolar disorder, but the magnitude of the benefit was small.26 On the basis of this finding, the Canadian Network for Mood and Anxiety Treatments (CANMAT) guidelines recommend treatment with an adjunctive serotonin reuptake inhibitor or bupropion as a second-line option for acute episodes of bipolar depression.27 However, given the absence of data from long-term placebo-controlled trials and a meta-analysis that showed an increased risk of the potential for switching to a manic or hypomanic episode,21 the CANMAT guidelines recommend discontinuing treatment with antidepressants within 8 weeks after remission of depression. The result of the primary-outcome analysis in this trial generally supports the CANMAT recommendation, because the times to mood episode did not differ significantly between the group that discontinued antidepressant therapy at 8 weeks and the group that continued therapy for 52 weeks.

A prespecified sensitivity analysis of the primary outcome and of the secondary outcome of a depressive episode suggested that continuing adjunctive antidepressant therapy for 52 weeks may prolong the time to a depressive relapse, but these analyses were not adjusted for multiplicity, and causal inferences cannot be drawn. Manic or hypomanic episodes were observed in $1 2 \%$ of the patients in the 52-week group and $6 \%$ of the patients in the 8-week group in the current trial; the confidence interval for the hazard ratio for time to a manic episode was wide and includes 1. The results for this and other secondary outcomes may be of interest to clinicians. In findings consistent with the results of this trial, a Swedish national registry study showed no increase in rates of switching to a manic state in patients who received treatment with antidepressants in conjunction with adjunctive mood stabilizers,28 and a study by the Stanley Foundation Bipolar Network showed a $1 3 \%$ rate of switching to manic or hypomanic episodes during 1 year of treatment with adjunctive antidepressants,29 an incidence similar to that observed in the 52- week group in the current trial.

This trial was designed to mimic real-world conditions as much as possible. Nevertheless, the trial has limitations. Recruitment was stopped before the planned sample size was reached owing to the Covid-19 pandemic and expiration of funding. Only $1 2 \%$ of trial participants were White and less than $1 \%$ were Black, which limits the generalizability of the findings. In addition, most patients in this trial were recruited from sites in India, so cultural, racial, and health care system differences may limit generalizability. The findings may not be applicable to treatment with antidepressants other than bupropion XL and escitalopram. Furthermore, the trial population was enriched for patients who had responses to these antidepressants.

In a trial that was stopped early owing to slow recruitment and funding limitations, adjunctive treatment with escitalopram or bupropion XL that continued for 52 weeks did not show a significant benefit as compared with 8 weeks of treatment with respect to the occurrence of any mood episode in patients with bipolar I disorder who had had recent remission of a depressive episode.
