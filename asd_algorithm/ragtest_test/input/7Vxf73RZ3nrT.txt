# Childhood Trauma Questionnaire-based child maltreatment profiles to predict efficacy of the Cognitive Behavioral Analysis System of Psychotherapy versus non-specific psychotherapy in adults with early-onset chronic depression: cluster analysis of data from a randomised controlled trial

Stephan Goerigk\*, Moritz Elsaesser\*, Matthias A Reinhard, Levente Kriston, Martin Härter, Martin Hautzinger, Jan Philipp Klein, James P McCullough Jr, Elisabeth Schramm†, Frank Padberg†

# Summary

Background Child maltreatment is a broadly confirmed risk factor for mental and physical illness. Some psychological treatments specifically target mental health conditions associated with child maltreatment. For example, the Cognitive Behavioral Analysis System of Psychotherapy (CBASP) focuses on maladaptive interpersonal behaviours in chronic depression. However, how the assessment of child maltreatment could inform personalised treatment is unclear. We used data from a previously published clinical trial to investigate whether a pre-established child maltreatment clustering approach predicts differential outcomes after CBASP versus non-specific supportive psychotherapy in patients with early-onset chronic depression.

Methods We did a cluster analysis of data from a previous randomised controlled trial of unmedicated adult outpatients with early-onset chronic depression who were treated at eight university clinics and psychological institutes in Germany with 32 sessions of CBASP or non-specific supportive psychotherapy. Participants were eligible for the original trial if they were aged 18–65 years; had major depressive disorder (MDD) with an early onset and duration of at least 2 years, current MDD superimposed on a pre-existing dysthymic disorder, or recurrent MDD with incomplete remission between episodes as defined by DSM-IV; and had a score of at least 20 points on the 24-item Hamilton Rating Scale for Depression (HRSD-24). Participants were included in the current study if they had completed the short form of the Childhood Trauma Questionnaire (CTQ) at trial baseline. We used an agglomerative hierarchical clustering approach to derive child maltreatment clusters from individual patterns across the five domains of the CTQ. We used linear mixed models to investigate whether clustering could predict differential clinical outcomes (change in symptom severity on the HRSD-24) up to 2 years after treatment onset. People with lived experience were involved in the current study.

# Research in context

# Evidence before this study

Child maltreatment constitutes a major risk factor for impaired mental and physical health and predicts adverse clinical outcomes (eg, chronic courses of depressive disorders). There is an ongoing debate about whether information on child maltreatment is useful in personalising diagnostics and treatment, such as psychological interventions. When using child maltreatment to predict treatment outcomes, clinical trials analyse either dichotomic information (ie, childhood trauma vs no childhood trauma), total child maltreatment scores, or scores of single child maltreatment domains. A transdiagnostic study (Goerigk and colleagues, 2023) adopted a novel Childhood Trauma Questionnaire (CTQ)-based clustering approach that considered combinations of child maltreatment subtypes and was able to predict adverse clinical outcomes in a primary and independent validation sample. We conducted a systematic literature search of MEDLINE via Ovid and PsycINFO databases using the search terms “adverse childhood experience(s)”, “child(hood) maltreatment”, “childhood trauma”, and “cluster”. There were no restrictions regarding the publication period or language. Additionally, backward and forward citation searches of relevant studies and reviews were performed. The literature search was last updated on June 14, 2024. We identified several studies that have attempted to identify child maltreatment subtypes. However, comparable approaches have not yet been used to predict responses to disorder-specific psychological treatment, including the Cognitive Behavioral Analysis System of Psychotherapy (CBASP), which has been hypothesised to be particularly effective in people living with chronic depression, who commonly have experienced maltreatment during childhood.

# Added value of this study

This study shows that child maltreatment clusters specifically predict the response to psychological therapy. We applied a pre-established CTQ-based clustering approach to the independent dataset of a multicentre randomised controlled trial that compared CBASP and non-specific supportive psychotherapy in a large sample of unmedicated adult outpatients with early-onset chronic depression. Child maltreatment clusters were predictive for large differences in treatment outcomes, with superiority of CBASP over supportive psychotherapy in three clusters (predominant emotional neglect, predominant emotional neglect and emotional abuse, and emotional neglect and emotional and physical abuse) over 48 weeks. These differences remained stable in two of three clusters over the 2-year follow-up period. Our findings support the notion that child maltreatment information is clinically meaningful at a degree of complexity reached by the child maltreatment clustering approach. To encourage dissemination and paradigmatically to show how the cluster model could support clinicians in their decision-making process, we provide a publicly available online application.

# Implications of all the available evidence

Current evidence encourages the implementation of more complex approaches in assessing and analysing child maltreatment to personalise psychological interventions in individuals who report adverse clinical outcomes associated with child maltreatment. The CTQ-based clustering approach could be suitable for clinical implementation because it helps to identify individuals with chronic depression who might particularly benefit from CBASP. These findings underline the importance of establishing and refining child maltreatmentbased trauma concepts in clinical practice.

However, procedures are often not disseminated into clinical practice due to incompatibility with clinical workflows, concerns about reliability and generalisability, and regulatory and ethical considerations.3

Broadly confirmed individual characteristics that constitute risk factors for adverse mental health conditions include child maltreatment,4 which is defined by WHO as physical abuse, neglect, emotional abuse, and sexual abuse. With a prevalence of $42 \%$ in retrospective estimates covering the first 18 years of life,5 child maltreatment is a major contributor to the global burden of disease and disability. Furthermore, child maltreatment is associated with more severe symptoms of mental health conditions, younger age of onset, higher numbers of comorbidities, and treatment resistance.6 For instance, evidence-based pharmacological and psychotherapeutic treatments show unfavourable results in people living with depression who have a history of child maltreatment, and these individuals are more likely to develop chronic or recurrent trajectories.7,8

Despite such overwhelming evidence for the importance of child maltreatment, there is uncertainty regarding its predictive value for allocating clinical interventions. A systematic review and meta-analysis of 29 interventional studies9 investigated the relevance of child maltreatment for the efficacy of pharmacotherapy and psychotherapy in depression by applying a dichotomous criterion (presence or absence) and reported similar symptom improvement for patients with and without childhood trauma after both types of treatment.

An explanation as to why child maltreatment is not taken into account in clinical decision paths might lie in the variation in approaches to the qualitative and quantitative assessment of child maltreatment. Usually, studies analyse dichotomous information, total scores, or scores on individual subscales.10 However, such approaches can lose relevant information because different forms of child maltreatment typically occur in combination with each other.10–12 In 2023, we addressed this topic by applying a clustering approach that considered both the co-occurrence and the severity of burden across individual child maltreatment domains.13 Specifically, we derived child maltreatment clusters from the five domains of the Childhood Trauma Questionnaire (CTQ),14 which is considered the goldstandard self-report scale for child maltreatment. Our main finding was that the child maltreatment clusters were superior to traditional diagnoses in predicting adverse clinical outcomes and robust to external validation.

In the field of psychological interventions, child maltreatment clinically informs trauma-focused therapies for the treatment of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) and complex PTSD (eg, traumafocused cognitive behavioural therapy or eye movement desensitisation and reprocessing), but information regarding child maltreatment is not routinely assessed in other conditions to guide psychological treatment, and interventions with a focus on child maltreatment are scarce.

One example of an existing intervention that takes child maltreatment into account is the Cognitive Behavioral Analysis System of Psychotherapy (CBASP), which has been specifically developed for the treatment of chronic depression.15 CBASP represents a highly structured, interpersonal learning-acquisition model focusing on the dyadic relationship between the patient and the therapist. By the use of disciplined personal involvement (ie, disclosure of the therapist’s emotional reaction to the patient’s behaviour), CBASP aims to connect the patient’s perception to their social environment, discriminating the therapist’s reactions from those of significant others (eg, the patient’s friends and family), and creating a sense of felt safety. Additionally, CBASP focuses on social problemsolving by situational analysis techniques to acquire new interpersonal behaviours and counteract interpersonal patterns that nurture persistent depression.16,17 After the first randomised controlled trial by Keller and colleagues,16 which compared CBASP, pharmacotherapy, and their combination, a secondary analysis by Nemeroff and colleagues18 indicated that CBASP was more effective than pharmacotherapy in treating patients with a history of child maltreatment.

In the study reported here, we aimed to use the preestablished clustering approach to predict differential treatment effects of CBASP by using data from an independent, multicentre, randomised controlled trial.17 We investigated whether patterns of co-occurring child maltreatment differentiate the efficacy of CBASP versus non-specific supportive psychotherapy in a sample of adult outpatients with early-onset chronic depression. Supportive psychotherapy was chosen for comparison in both the trial and current study because it represents an active, non-specific, common-factors psychological intervention. Secondary analyses of the trial found evidence that CBASP was superior to supportive psychotherapy in patients with a history of emotional abuse; however, findings regarding the overall effect of child maltreatment were inconsistent.19,20 Thus, we hypothesised that including CTQ-based information at a degree of complexity that reflects particular aspects of child maltreatment (ie, considering both severity and co-occurrence of subtypes: emotional neglect, emotional abuse, physical neglect, physical abuse, and sexual abuse) would reveal a more comprehensive pattern of the differential treatment efficacy of CBASP versus non-specific psychotherapy.

# Methods

#

In the cluster analysis and efficacy analyses reported here, we used data from a previous multicentre, evaluator-masked, parallel-group, randomised controlled trial that recruited and treated participants between March 5, 2010, and Oct 14, 2013, at eight university clinics and psychological institutes in Germany (appendix p 2). The study design, rationale, and protocol of the original trial are described in detail elsewhere.17,21 The study was approved by the ethics committee of the University of Freiburg (Freiburg im Breisgau, Germany; approval reference 240/09) and the local ethics committees of all partner sites. The trial was registered with ClinicalTrials. gov (NCT00970437).

The head of the German contact point of self-help groups for a wide range of topics was part of the steering committee of the original trial, participating in all key processes and decisions regarding study design and implementation. Furthermore, some study personnel (including some of the authors of the current study) have personal experiences with depression, child maltreatment, or both and are involved in the current study as lived-experience researchers.

# Participants

Adult outpatients were eligible for the original randomised controlled trial if they were aged 18–65 years; had major depressive disorder (MDD) with an early onset (ie, before age 21 years) and duration of at least 2 years, current MDD superimposed on a pre-existing dysthymic disorder, or recurrent MDD with incomplete remission between episodes as defined by DSM-IV; and a score of at least 20 points on the 24-item Hamilton Rating Scale for Depression (HRSD-24).17,21,22 Sex was self reported with options of female, male, and other. Participants were prohibited from using antidepressant medication during the trial; those taking antidepressants were given the option of discontinuing $_ { \mathrm { \Omega } } { \geq } 2$ weeks’ washout) before enrolment. Written informed consent was obtained from all participants. Trial participants were included in the current study if they had completed the short form of the CTQ at trial baseline.

# Randomisation and masking

In the original randomised controlled trial, patients were randomly assigned to receive 24 acute sessions of CBASP or supportive psychotherapy for 20 weeks, followed by eight continuation sessions over 28 weeks. A computergenerated block randomisation sequence was used with randomly varying block size, stratified for trial site.17,21 The randomisation sequence was prepared by LK, the trial statistician, who was not involved in recruitment and was masked to treatment allocation during the analysis of the primary and secondary outcomes. HRSD-24 ratings were performed by trained, experienced clinicians who were masked to treatment allocation; inter-rater reliability of the HRSD-24 has been shown to be excellent.17

# Procedures

In the original randomised controlled trial, patients in both groups received a total of 32 psychotherapy sessions according to standardised treatment manuals.15,17,21 In the acute phase, patients participated in two sessions a week for the first 4 weeks and then one session a week for the remaining 16 weeks, making a total of 24 acute sessions. Then, over the next 28 weeks, they attended two sessions in the first 4 weeks and one session per month thereafter, making a total of eight continuation sessions. Supportive psychotherapy was based on a pre-existing therapeutic manual designed as an active, non-specific psychotherapeutic intervention that resembled client-centred counselling.23 It incorporated psychoeducational components and other typical elements of psychotherapy including reflective listening, fostering emotional expression, helping the patient to feel acknowledged, showing empathy, instilling hope, and embracing therapeutic optimism. A description of the CBASP model and a comparison of CBASP and supportive psychotherapy elements are included in the appendix $\mathrm { ( p p ~ } 3 \mathrm { - } 4 )$ . In the current study, individuals were grouped into child maltreatment clusters derived from individuals’ scores on the five domains of the short form of the CTQ, and outcomes were analysed by treatment group (CBASP vs supportive psychotherapy) and child maltreatment cluster.

# Outcomes

The primary objective of the current study was to derive child maltreatment clusters based on scores on the five domains of the CTQ, and to assess whether the clustering approach could predict differential outcomes of depression severity as measured on the clinician-rated HRSD-24, the primary outcome of the original randomised controlled trial. In the original trial, child maltreatment was measured at baseline with the short form of the CTQ, which encompasses 25 items (excluding three minimisation and denial items) rated on a 5-point Likert scale (1=never true to $5 \mathrm { = }$ very often true; appendix p 5).14 Notably, the CTQ distinguishes emotional and physical neglect, measuring five dimensions of child maltreatment with five items each (5–25 points; emotional neglect, physical neglect, emotional abuse, physical abuse, and sexual abuse), unlike WHO’s approach, which assesses four child maltreatment subcategories (physical abuse, neglect, emotional abuse, and sexual abuse).

Secondary outcomes from the original trial were also analysed by treatment group and child maltreatment cluster in the current study. These outcomes included response $( \ge 5 0 \%$ improvement on the HRSD-24 from baseline), remission (HRSD-24 score ${ \leq } 8$ ), and scores on the self-rated Inventory of Depressive Symptomatology, Global Assessment of Functioning scale, 12-item ShortForm Health Survey (SF-12), and Quality of Life in Depression Scale. For detailed descriptions of these scales, see the published study protocol.21 Participants were assessed before being randomly assigned in the original study (on average 2 weeks before the start of treatment), at treatment onset, after 12 and 20 weeks of acute treatment, and at the end of extended treatment (ie, after 48 weeks). Follow-up measurements of the HRSD-24 were performed after 1 year and 2 years and the change from baseline until each of these follow-ups was analysed as a secondary endpoint.

# Statistical analysis

As the input for an agglomerative hierarchical clustering algorithm (stats::hclust function), the five CTQ subscales were used to group individuals into child maltreatment clusters analogous to those described by Goerigk and colleagues.13 This procedure sequentially groups patients according to the similarity of their responses to the CTQ, and groups of individuals that merge at high values relative to the merge points of their subgroups are considered candidates for belonging to the same cluster. Hierarchical clustering can reveal structures in data without previously made assumptions about the number of clusters and provides deterministic solutions. Proximity between individuals was quantified with the Ward error sum of squares agglomeration method on the Euclidean distance metric.2 The optimal number of clusters was established by an adaptive branch-pruning procedure for hierarchical clustering dendrograms with the hybrid method, and minimal cluster sizes of one.24

Efficacy analyses were performed analogous to those described by Schramm and colleagues.17 Change in continuous outcomes was evaluated with linear mixed models, and repeated measurements were assumed to be nested within patients. No imputation was performed because linear mixed models handle missing data using maximum likelihood estimation, assuming missingness at random, conditional on information in the model. # Discussion

This study investigated whether a pre-established CTQbased clustering method could differentially predict the clinical outcome of CBASP compared with supportive psychotherapy in the independent dataset of a large randomised controlled trial enrolling unmedicated patients with early-onset chronic depression. CTQbased clustering identified seven clusters, which differentially predicted the efficacy of CBASP versus supportive psychotherapy with superior effects for CBASP in patients with more complex and more severe child maltreatment. Specifically, CBASP was superior to supportive psychotherapy in clusters type 4 (predominant emotional neglect), type 5 (predominant emotional neglect and emotional abuse), and type 6 (emotional neglect and emotional and physical abuse). For types 5 and 6, the differential efficacy was maintained over the follow-up period of 2 years.

These findings support our hypothesis that patterns of co-occurring burden across the child maltreatment domains of emotional neglect, emotional abuse, physical neglect, physical abuse, and sexual abuse are clinically informative in terms of treatment prediction.

In this respect, both qualitative and quantitative information on child maltreatment might be important. Our findings also indicate that the differential treatment outcomes in the original trial $( d { = } 0 \cdot 3 9 ) ^ { 1 7 }$ could be improved by personalising treatment selection according to child maltreatment patterns. Specifically, patients in cluster types 4–6 responded significantly better to CBASP (with large effect sizes, with $d$ ranging from 0·98 to 1·54), whereas patients in types $^ { 1 - 3 }$ benefited similarly from CBASP and supportive psychotherapy. In former secondary analyses of this randomised controlled trial,17 effects were found only for the subdomain of emotional abuse, with moderate effect sizes after treatment and 1 year of follow-up.19,20

Our findings align with the chronic depression model of McCullough,15 highlighting the putative relevance of CBASP and its psychotherapeutic techniques that address child maltreatment. The superiority of CBASP in clusters with three different types of co-occurring child maltreatment (emotional neglect, emotional abuse, and physical abuse) supports the need to investigate interactions between these child maltreatment domains and domain-specific action of CBASP techniques. Importantly, this study addressed the effect of targeting maladaptive interpersonal behaviours associated with child maltreatment in a sample of patients with chronic depression and early onset. Furthermore, our analyses might be generalisable to the population of chronically depressed patients because rates and patterns of child maltreatment in the present sample closely resemble those found in earlier studies.26,27

Despite the obvious relevance of child maltreatment for informing treatments, its integration into mental health-care routines still needs development. We advocate the conduct of child maltreatment assessments in clinically supportive settings, prioritising patient-led choices and ensuring informed consent, in line with best practices for trauma-informed care.28 By providing an open-source online application that predicts the child maltreatment cluster of a new patient with a multinomial regression model that is fit to data observed in this trial, we encourage the dissemination of our approach. However, prospective randomised controlled trials are warranted to test its clinical usefulness in different settings.

Our study has several limitations. First, the measurement of child maltreatment was based on retrospective self-report measures, which might be prone to effects of memory and subjective appraisal. However, a 2023 study suggested that subjective experiences of child maltreatment are more predictive of current and lifetime adverse clinical outcomes (ie, symptoms of depression and anxiety) than objective records of child maltreatment.29 Second, child maltreatment might be viewed as part of the broader concept of adverse childhood experiences, which encompasses other forms of early-life adversity including household dysfunction, parental loss or separation, witnessing inter-partner violence, or bullying victimisation. Although these types of adverse childhood experiences are not captured by the CTQ, they also constitute severe traumatising factors and might be associated with child maltreatment domains. In addition, the timing of child maltreatment could have clinical significance but is not assessed by the CTQ. Future research should therefore investigate whether this information can be used to refine the child maltreatment cluster model. Third, PTSD symptoms were not assessed beyond the diagnostic categories in the original randomised controlled trial. Fourth, nonspecific supportive psychotherapy might be regarded as a limited active treatment for chronic depression. Interestingly, supportive psychotherapy was equally effective as CBASP in patients in clusters with no or low burden of child maltreatment (ie, types 1–3), supporting the notion that it was a suitable therapy, not specific for child maltreatment, for use as a comparator. Fifth, cluster sizes varied considerably, and we cannot exclude the possibility of false-negative findings for single clusters. Finally, child maltreatment clusters need further theoretical and clinical anchoring because the CTQ-based approach was mainly data-driven.

Although our findings cannot be generalised beyond CBASP versus non-specific supportive psychotherapy, the predictive value of the child maltreatment cluster model warrants further investigation and comparisons with other psychological interventions (eg, CBT) and psychodynamic therapies. In addition, the approach could be used to compare the efficacy of psychological therapies and pharmacotherapy.9,18 Another objective involves systematically benchmarking the cluster model and other CTQ-based measures (eg, single subscale and total scores) to evaluate their predictive capacities in different clinical contexts. In this respect, the child maltreatment clusters show potential not only for prediction, but also for differentiating individuals on the basis of combinations of child maltreatment, which might have clinical significance. For instance, types 4–7 (ie, predominant emotional neglect, predominant emotional neglect and abuse, emotional neglect and emotional and physical abuse, emotional neglect and emotional and sexual abuse) represent distinct traumatising experiences beyond varying degrees of severity. Considering this level of complexity might change practices of child maltreatment assessment in clinical care and enhance individualisation of psychotherapy. Although there has been impressive progress in understanding of the neurobiological underpinnings of depression in relation to child maltreatment,4 this progress has not been systematically incorporated in psychotherapy research. The cluster model might provide insights regarding neurobiological subtypes associated with child maltreatment, offering additional information compared with a unidimensional severity model. Finally, although our results support the efficacy of CBASP in early-onset chronic depression, their application for treating psychological sequelae of child maltreatment in other mental health conditions represents a novel research avenue.

In conclusion, our study provides evidence that patterns of recalled experiences of child maltreatment across the domains of emotional neglect, emotional abuse, physical neglect, physical abuse, and sexual abuse allow the identification of patients with earlyonset chronic depression and a particular burden of child maltreatment who could specifically benefit from CBASP above non-specific psychotherapy. This study could guide clinical decision making and patient-led choices about the selection of therapeutic interventions. Our results underline the importance of refining current child maltreatment-based trauma concepts4,30 and could support the dissemination of psychological approaches that recognise the complexity of child maltreatment.

Proposals should be directed to elisabeth.schramm@uniklinik-freiburg.de; to obtain access to the data, requesters will need to sign a data access agreement. Requests for the statistical analysis plan and the analytical code should be directed to stephan.goerigk@med.uni-muenchen.de.
